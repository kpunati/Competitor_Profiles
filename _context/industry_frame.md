# Industry Frame: Wealth Management & Financial Planning

*Background context the analyzer should use when reading any competitor in this category. Sharpen this as we learn more from actual profiles.*

*Last updated: 2026-05-11*

---

## The competitive set, in rough buckets

- **Wirehouses / bank-owned advisors** — Morgan Stanley, Merrill, UBS, JPM Wealth. Brand and scale. Tied to product shelves. Often opaque on fees.
- **Independent RIAs (registered investment advisors)** — fiduciary by structure, fee-only or fee-based. Range from solo practitioners to billion-dollar firms. Our category.
- **Hybrid digital advisors** — Vanguard PAS, Schwab Intelligent Portfolios Premium, Fidelity Wealth Services. Algorithmic core, human layer on top, low fees, scale-driven.
- **Pure robo-advisors** — Betterment, Wealthfront, Ellevest, SoFi Invest. Software-only, very cheap, weak on complex situations.
- **Holistic financial planning networks** — Edelman Financial Engines, Creative Planning, Mariner Wealth, Carson Group. Aggressive growth, often acquired-and-rolled-up RIAs.
- **Niche / segment-focused firms** — by profession (doctors, tech employees), life stage (women, near-retirees), values (ESG, faith-based).

## What's universally true in this category

- **Regulatory weight.** Most US firms serving individuals are SEC- or state-registered RIAs and file Form ADV publicly. ADV Part 2A discloses AUM, services, fee schedules, disciplinary history, conflicts of interest. This is a free, comprehensive, mandated source of company facts — check it.
- **Trust is the product.** Visual identity skews conservative: blues, grays, traditional serifs or stable sans-serifs, real-people photography, lots of credentials and titles.
- **Compliance compresses copy.** Marketing language is generally cautious — heavy disclaimers, "may," "could," "past performance" footnotes everywhere. Firms that sound human stand out.
- **Pricing is mostly gated.** Almost no one publishes fee schedules on the marketing site. Most lead with "schedule a consultation." Transparency on price is itself a positioning move.
- **CTAs are heavy.** "Schedule a call," "Talk to an advisor," "Get matched." Self-serve signup is rare and signals a robo or DIY tool, not a wealth management firm.

## Patterns to look for and flag

- **Who is their hero customer?** Pictured, named, demographic — and how it differs from their words. A firm whose photography is all silver-haired couples but whose copy talks about "your career" is at odds with itself.
- **What does the language reveal about fiduciary posture?** "Fiduciary" stated outright vs. only implied vs. avoided.
- **AUM minimums** — usually disclosed in ADV even if hidden on site. A strong signal for who the firm is really for.
- **Tech posture.** Mention of "client portal," "planning software," "tax-loss harvesting," "direct indexing" → modern. No mention → traditional.
- **Segment claims.** "We serve everyone" vs. "We specialize in X." Niches usually convert better; broad claims are easier to find but harder to win.
- **The fee disclosure pattern.** Flat fee / AUM % / hourly / commission — and whether they say it on the site or hide it behind a call.

## What "good" tends to look like for an independent RIA going to market today

- A clear segment claim (or an explicit segmented site with separate paths)
- Transparent or near-transparent fees
- Modern but trust-coded visual identity — not boring, not flashy
- Real human voice in the copy, real advisors named and photographed
- Evidence of modern tooling visible to the prospect (planning portal, dashboard screenshots, etc.)
- ADV findings clean or explained
- Content that meets prospects where they are by segment (e.g., a 401(k) rollover guide for the mid-career segment, an "investing in your 20s" piece for the early-adult segment)

## What tends to look bad (and is therefore the easy gap to fill)

- Generic stock photography of skylines, handshakes, mountain climbers
- "Wealth management for the modern investor" with no specifics about *which* modern investor
- No fee disclosure anywhere on site
- No real advisor bios, or bios that are all credentials and no personality
- A blog last updated in 2022
- Identical messaging for clearly different segments
