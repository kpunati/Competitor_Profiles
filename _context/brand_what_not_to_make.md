# United Success — What NOT to Make

> **Purpose of this document:** to specify the patterns, clichés, and stylistic choices that DO NOT belong in United Success marketing material. The Brand Brief tells Claude what we are. This document tells Claude what we are not.
>
> **Why this exists:** every wealth management firm uses the same generic visual and verbal templates. Avoiding those templates is half of what makes our brand recognizable. This document catalogs the patterns to avoid.

---

## 1. VISUAL CLICHÉS WE AVOID

### Stock photography
- Two business people shaking hands across a desk
- A diverse group of professionals pointing at a laptop
- A man in a suit standing at a window looking thoughtfully at a city skyline (we use a real skyscraper image, but as a background overlay — not as the focal subject)
- A woman in a blazer reviewing documents with a serious expression
- A family on a beach silhouetted at sunset
- A retired couple holding hands and walking on a beach
- Hands typing on a calculator next to charts and graphs
- Gold scales of justice or weights
- Lightbulb above an open laptop
- Climbing graphs that go to infinity
- Anyone wearing a Bluetooth headset
- "Generic happy retiree" photography of any kind

### Generic financial iconography
- Three little person icons in a row (representing "team")
- A piggy bank
- A vault or safe
- A handshake icon
- Coins stacked vertically
- A house with a dollar sign
- A graduation cap with money
- A retirement palm tree
- A green up-arrow
- A simple line chart graphic with no real data
- "Trust" / "Integrity" / "Excellence" as a three-icon row

### Color palettes to avoid
- Pure black with neon gold (too crypto / too aggressive)
- Forest green with brown (too "old-money tax accountant")
- Burgundy with cream (too "law firm from 1985")
- Pastel anything (too "lifestyle brand")
- Any palette without warm gold (gold is non-negotiable)

### Layouts to avoid
- Five equal feature boxes in a row with icons
- "Choose your plan" tier tables (we're not SaaS)
- Step-by-step process diagrams that read "Discovery → Plan → Implement → Review"
- Infographic-style anything (we're editorial, not BuzzFeed)
- Carousels with autoplay
- Hero section with a giant photo of someone smiling at the camera
- Slanted/skewed section dividers
- Anything that looks like Squarespace's "Bedford" template

### Typography to avoid
- ALL CAPS HEADLINES IN SANS-SERIF (we use Playfair italic for emphasis)
- Body copy in serif (we use Inter sans for body, Playfair only for headers/emphasis)
- Gradients on body text
- Letter-spacing under 0px on tight headlines (compresses too far)
- Default Google Fonts pairings like Roboto + Open Sans
- Comic Sans (obvious)
- Anything that defaults to whatever WordPress theme is loaded

### Motion to avoid
- Spinning logos or rotating elements
- Bouncing arrows
- Auto-scrolling content carousels
- Hero videos that loop a stock clip of a city
- Parallax effects on phones
- Anything that demands attention vs. rewards attention
- Hover effects on every single element

## 2. VERBAL CLICHÉS WE AVOID

### Empty marketing phrases
- "Industry-leading"
- "World-class"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Top-tier"
- "Premier"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "Innovative"
- "Robust"
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Synergy" (in any form)
- "Holistic" (overused in advisor marketing — it's lost its meaning)
- "Comprehensive" (be specific instead)
- "Solutions" (as a generic noun — "we provide solutions to your needs")
- "Bespoke" / "white-glove" / "concierge" (cliché)
- "Boutique" (overused by every small firm)

### Boastful claims without substance
- "We have decades of combined experience" (be specific: who, where, what)
- "Our team of experts" (name them, give credentials)
- "We are passionate about" (everyone says this)
- "Your trusted partner" (every firm says this)
- "Personalized service" (assume yes — show it, don't say it)
- "Putting clients first" (legally required as a fiduciary — saying it is meaningless)
- "Custom-tailored to your needs" (filler)
- "Going above and beyond" (filler)
- "We treat you like family" (concerning, actually)

### Generic financial advisor copy
- "Build wealth"
- "Achieve your financial goals"
- "Secure your future"
- "Plan for tomorrow, today"
- "Your journey to financial freedom"
- "Take control of your financial future"
- "Make your money work for you"
- "Smart investing for smart investors"
- "Financial peace of mind"

### Vague claims about process
- "We start with a deep discovery process"
- "We take a comprehensive approach"
- "We provide ongoing support"
- "We're with you every step of the way"
- "Open communication is at the heart of our practice"

## 3. STRUCTURAL ANTI-PATTERNS

### "Our Values" section with three generic values
The generic version every firm has:
- Integrity
- Excellence
- Client-first

Replace with: convictions that have specific behaviors attached. We do this on the About page (trend is your friend / risk first / we'll tell you if not a fit).

### "Meet the Team" page with cropped headshots and generic bios
The generic version:
- Square cropped photo
- Name in bold
- Title under name
- Two paragraphs that start with "John brings over 20 years of experience..."

Replace with: editorial profile treatment with credentials prominent, career history as a credit list, real specialization (not generic), and direct contact info.

### "Our Process" with four-step diagram
The generic version:
1. Discovery
2. Plan
3. Implement
4. Monitor

Replace with: an honest description of what actually happens. Or skip entirely — process diagrams are filler.

### "Why Choose Us" with checkmark bullet list
The generic version:
- ✓ Personalized service
- ✓ Decades of experience
- ✓ Comprehensive approach
- ✓ Putting clients first

Replace with: conviction statements, specific differentiators, or skip entirely.

### Generic FAQ section
The generic FAQ that has answers like:
- "We charge based on assets under management"
- "We work with high-net-worth families"
- "Our process is collaborative"

Replace with: FAQs that lead with the biggest unspoken objection a prospect actually has — and answer it honestly, even when the honest answer is uncomfortable.

## 4. PHOTOGRAPHIC DIRECTION

### When using real photos
- Real Kean and Beata photos when we have them
- Office shots that look like an actual office, not a staged set
- Real Chatham, NJ shots that feel local, not "anywhere USA"

### When using stock imagery
- The skyscraper image we already use as low-opacity texture overlay is fine — but only as texture, not as a hero subject
- Avoid anything that depicts specific people doing financial advisor activities
- Subtle texture (paper grain, light noise) over color is preferred to literal imagery
- Abstract gradients with gold tones are preferred to literal photographs

### What we don't depict
- Specific dollar amounts
- Specific market predictions
- Stock tickers (too transactional, we're not a trading platform)
- Bull or bear imagery (clichéd — and we manage both directions)

## 5. TONE ANTI-PATTERNS

### Don't sound like a startup
- "We're disrupting the wealth management industry"
- "We're rethinking how financial advice works"
- "The future of investing is here"
- Em dashes used like commas
- Lowercase everything for "design" reasons
- Quirky personality / dad jokes / casual workplace speak

### Don't sound like a bank
- "We are pleased to inform you"
- "Our valued clients"
- "It is our pleasure to serve you"
- "Esteemed clientele"
- Anything that reads like it was approved by a compliance committee at JPMorgan

### Don't sound like a robo-advisor
- "Optimize your portfolio"
- "Algorithm-driven returns"
- "Set it and forget it"
- "Diversification at scale"
- Any framing that implies our work is automated

### Don't sound like a SaaS company
- "Get started today"
- "Try us free for 30 days"
- "Onboarding flow"
- "Premium features"
- "Tier upgrade"

### Don't sound desperate
- "Call now!"
- "Limited time offer"
- "Don't miss out"
- "Spaces filling fast"
- Multiple exclamation points
- "We can help you achieve [generic goal]" (assume the prospect knows their goal)

## 6. CTA ANTI-PATTERNS

### Generic CTAs we avoid
- "Learn More" (we have specific verbs available)
- "Click Here"
- "Submit"
- "Get Started"
- "Sign Up"
- "Subscribe to our newsletter" (we don't currently have one)

### CTAs that pressure
- "Book before slots fill up"
- "Limited consultations available this month"
- "Don't let another quarter go by"

### Use these instead
- "Schedule a Consultation"
- "Book Your Consultation"
- "Let's Talk"
- "Speak with Our Team"
- "Speak with Our Team Directly"
- "Reach Us" (for contact info)

## 7. WHEN IN DOUBT — TEST QUESTIONS

Before publishing any piece of material, run it through these:

### Verbal check
- Could this be copy-pasted onto any other RIA's website without changes? If yes — rewrite.
- Does it use specific names, credentials, locations, claims? If no — add them.
- Could a 12-year-old explain what this says? If no — simplify.
- Did I use "your trusted partner" anywhere? If yes — delete.

### Visual check
- Could this be a Squarespace template? If yes — make it more editorial.
- Does it use gold and navy? If no — does it need to?
- Is there at least one italic Playfair moment? If no — is this a piece where one would fit?
- Are there real numbers/credentials/claims? If no — add them.

### Tone check
- Does it sound like a real person wrote it? If no — rewrite.
- Does it acknowledge any tradeoff or limitation? If no — should it?
- Is there at least one place where we sound like an honest broker (telling someone they might not be a fit)? If the piece is long enough to warrant it — yes.

---

## 8. ONE-LINE SUMMARY

**If it looks or sounds like every other wealth management firm — start over.**

Our entire brand is built on NOT being that. Every choice that makes the material more specific, more honest, more editorially confident, and more visually distinct is the right choice.
