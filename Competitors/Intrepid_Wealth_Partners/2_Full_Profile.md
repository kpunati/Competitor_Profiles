# Intrepid Wealth Partners — Full Profile

*This is the deep dive. For a quick read, see `1_Summary.md`.*

## The big picture

Intrepid Wealth Partners is what a high-effort *solo virtual-advisor* practice looks like in 2026. One CFP, one EA, one contract compliance partner, 356 client accounts, $54M AUM, and a brand identity that goes much harder than the firm's size would predict. Founder Derek Notman ("DJ") is simultaneously the sole advisor, the firm's content output, and a separately positioned tech founder (Couplr AI) — a profile that's structurally closer to a creator-economy business than to a traditional RIA. The surf-themed copy ("beach vibes," "Catch Your Wave," tiers named The Break / The Set / The Barrel) is not decorative — it's the entire trust-building apparatus replacing what a multi-advisor firm would do with team photos and credential walls.

From our perspective as a pre-launch independent RIA, the most useful thing about Intrepid isn't the firm itself but the *isolated demonstrations* it provides of three category-level positioning moves we want to occupy: a fully published fee schedule, a named (not asserted) modern tech stack, and a contrarian-vs-conventional opening frame. They have made those moves in isolation, on top of a firm too small to defend them at scale. We can pick them up and put them at the center of a larger structure.

What Intrepid is *not* doing — and this is most of the entry opening — is serving anyone in our two target segments meaningfully. The "High-Earning Millennials" header is decorative, not strategic; what Intrepid actually wants is the millennial *who already has assets* — the doctor, the founder, the executive. The 26-year-old setting up a first investment account and the 45-year-old optimizing a 401(k) are both invisible to this firm's positioning.

## Who they sell to

Intrepid's homepage lists six target segments under the H2 "Who We Work With":

1. **High-Earning Millennials**
2. **Doctors**
3. **Founders**
4. **Executives**
5. **Business Owners**
6. **Surfers & Mountain Climbers**

The sixth is a tell — it's the personality screen, not a segment. The first five resolve to a single underlying ICP: **mid-career professionals or business operators with accumulated investable assets**, not first-time investors. The pricing page reinforces this: "We work with a limited number of high-performing clients and families ready to delegate complexity." The framing is "delegate complexity," which presupposes the prospect has complexity worth delegating — i.e., already-meaningful assets.

The math supports the same read: 356 accounts at $54M = ~$151K average. That's a long tail of smaller The Break / The Set clients with some larger Barrel accounts pulling the mean up. It's not a millennial-first-Roth book.

Evidence that the millennial framing is decorative, not strategic:
- The "high-earning" qualifier is doing all the work in "High-Earning Millennials" — they're not pitching to millennials, they're pitching to high-earners who happen to be millennial-aged.
- No content surface, free tool, or funnel for anyone in account-setup or savings-rate-decision mode.
- No held-away-401(k) story.
- "Personality minimums" presupposes the prospect has already cleared an asset bar (otherwise the framing is "no minimums" full stop).

## What they sell

A single product: discretionary fiduciary investment management with light financial planning, delivered virtually with periodic review meetings. The pricing page is the most informative single document on the site — three named tiers (The Break / The Set / The Barrel) gated by **investable assets**, with the **same** asset-based fee schedule across all tiers and incremental services bolted on as you move up:

| Tier | Target client | Service inclusions |
|---|---|---|
| **The Break** | <$100K investable assets | Goal-setting + customized roadmap; cash-flow & budgeting; Asset Map report (insurance, retirement, debt, college); portfolio risk & fee analysis; basic portfolio construction; 2 virtual review meetings/yr; unlimited email; referrals to vetted pros |
| **The Set** | $100K–$500K investable assets | Everything in The Break + custom portfolio construction; retirement/savings goal readiness; risk and insurance policy review |
| **The Barrel** | $500K+ investable assets | Everything in The Break and The Set + tax opportunities snapshot; legacy and legal preparedness check |

**Fee schedule** (identical across tiers, charged monthly, disclosed openly):
- $0 – $999,999: **1.00%**
- $1M – $2,999,999: **0.70%**
- $3M+: **0.55%**

Additional fees noted for "third-party fund expenses or extraordinary services." No flat-fee planning option, no advice-only option, no hourly. No account minimums; "we don't have account minimums, but we do have personality minimums."

**Buyer onboarding**: a single contact form at `/get-in-touch` titled "Help us get to know you" with qualitative questions ("What prompted you to reach out?", "Why do you want to work with Intrepid?", "How did you hear about us?"). Framing: "We work with a select group of motivated people and only open 12 new client relationships per quarter."

The 12-per-quarter framing matters strategically: at a stated cap of 48 new relationships/year against an existing book of 356 accounts, the firm projects 13% annual relationship growth max — but the actual capacity ceiling is the single advisor's time, which is already past the conventional solo-CFP envelope.

## The story they tell

Intrepid positions itself as **the anti-advisor advisor** — modern, transparent, virtual, evidence-based, technology-fluent, fee-disclosed, and personality-first. The implicit competitor is the broker-dealer-affiliated traditional advisor: commission-driven, opaque, salesy, locked into a product shelf. The whole brand is constructed against that imagined opponent. The contrarian frame ("the exact opposite of when you hear the term 'financial advisor'") is the firm's clearest single positioning sentence.

The secondary story is **lifestyle-aligned advice from a peer**. Derek's About-page bio (surfing, cars, world travel, dog ownership, chess with Branson, serial entrepreneurship) explicitly positions him as similar-to-client — "We're independent advisors, CFP® professionals, and entrepreneurs — just like many of our clients." The pitch is: I am one of you, I built this for people like you, I won't talk down to you.

The third layer is **scarcity as quality signal**. "We work with a limited number of high-performing clients," "only open 12 new client relationships per quarter," "personality minimums" — every framing reinforces that hiring Intrepid is something the client earns rather than buys.

This is a personality-economy positioning move, not an institutional one. It would not survive Derek leaving.

## What they promise

The hierarchy of promises a visitor sees:

1. **Primary** (the H1 above the fold): "**Fiduciary Investment Management** — For Driven Professionals and Entrepreneurs. Independent, evidence-based portfolios built for growth and peace of mind."
2. **Secondary**: client-segment validation ("Who We Work With" — millennials/doctors/founders/executives/business owners/surfers); client-want validation ("What our clients want" — money working hard, customized strategy, minimize taxes, fiduciary advice, digital experience, risk-adjusted returns); client-problem validation ("Problems our clients face" — investments, fees, risk, taxes, dreams & goals).
3. **Tertiary**: "We're your Guide" (positioning as alongside-you peer); "Your Investments are Our Focus" (three pillars: investment strategy, risk management, tax efficiency); "The best tech for the best experience" (Altruist / Asset Map / Nitrogen); the contrarian closer ("exact opposite of 'financial advisor'") + personality minimums.

## How they price

Pricing is **fully transparent** — exceptional for this category. The pricing page is the single most useful page on the site for our purposes. All three tier definitions and the underlying fee schedule are visible without gating, and "no account minimums" is stated explicitly.

The interesting nuance: the tiers are about **service depth**, not **fee level**. Every tier pays the same 1.00% / 0.70% / 0.55% asset-based fee — the differences are which planning deliverables you receive (basic portfolio vs. custom portfolio; tax-opportunity snapshot only on the top tier). That's an unusual structure. Most tiered pricing pages either (a) charge different fees per tier or (b) bundle services into flat-fee tiers. Intrepid keeps the fee schedule constant and varies *only* what they do for the money. The implicit message: lower-tier clients get the asset-management product; higher-tier clients get asset management plus planning depth.

The anchor strategy is **The Set** (the middle tier, $100K–$500K) — it's the tier with "custom portfolio construction" added, and the math says it's where the majority of the firm's average account sits.

## What they believe

The underlying worldview, reconstructed from copy:

1. **The financial-advisor industry is broken because it's commission-driven, opaque, and intimidating.** The contrarian opener and the "personality minimums" framing both encode this.
2. **Modern technology can replicate (or exceed) the comfort of a downtown wealth-management office** — the named Altruist/Asset Map/Nitrogen stack is the proof. You don't need to drive somewhere to feel served well.
3. **Evidence-based portfolio construction beats active stock-picking.** "Independent, evidence-based portfolios" in the hero; the H1 itself is fiduciary investment management, not market-timing or theme-picking.
4. **The advisor's job is to be a peer who has solved this for themselves and now solves it for you.** Derek's bio is the firm's credentialing — he is positioned as "one of you" rather than "above you."
5. **Pricing transparency is a moat.** They show fees because they believe most peers won't, and customers who care about that will choose the firm that does.

## What this means for our entry

### What's working for them

- **Published fee schedule on an indexed pricing page**, with the bands shown as a real table rather than gated behind a contact form. This is the single most studyable artifact on the site for our purposes.
- **Named tech stack on the homepage** (Altruist / Asset Map / Nitrogen) — turns a generic "modern tech" assertion into a verifiable claim. Altruist is named *and* linked from the navigation as the client login.
- **Direct IAPD link in the footer** ("CLICK HERE FOR OUR DISCLOSURES" → adviserinfo.sec.gov). Costs nothing, signals confidence, takes a regulatory requirement and turns it into a trust move.
- **Contrarian-vs-conventional positioning sentence** — "We're the exact opposite of when you hear the term 'financial advisor' (in a good way)" — is one of the cleanest single-sentence positioning statements in the catalog.
- **Capacity-cap scarcity language** — "only open 12 new client relationships per quarter" — pre-qualifies inbound and frames the firm as something the client earns.
- **Single-product simplicity.** One service, three depth tiers, one CTA pattern. The site is small and decisive.
- **No-minimum + personality-minimum** framing is genuinely clever — it converts a competitive disadvantage (no asset gate to filter prospects) into a personality screen.

### Where they're vulnerable

- **Single-practitioner capacity ceiling.** 1 advisor / 356 accounts is well past the conventional solo envelope. Growth either breaks service depth or breaks the brand (hiring breaks "personality minimums").
- **Key-person risk is total.** The brand and the operating capacity are both Derek. No succession plan visible.
- **The "Introductary" typo on the primary CTA** appears on every captured page. For a firm whose pitch is meticulous fiduciary care and modern professionalism, a misspelled primary button is a real wart. (May not be a typo to the firm — could be intentional brand voice — but it reads as an error.)
- **No content engine** despite a personality-led positioning. Two visible blog posts. The lever every comparable firm in our catalog uses to outwork the category (Ben Felix's YouTube at PWL, Ritholtz's everything, Streamline's Dave Zoller on YouTube) isn't pulled here.
- **Six-segment list dilutes any single-segment depth.** Compare Foundry's single ICP sentence — Intrepid lists six segments and ends up generic.
- **No 401(k) or early-adult surface at all.** The "high-earning millennial" framing is decorative; there is no funnel for someone setting up a first account or optimizing a held-away 401(k).
- **Site/regulatory copy discrepancy** (footer says "State Registered" while IAPD shows SEC file 801). For a firm whose pitch is pricing/regulatory transparency, an unresolved registration framing is a credibility risk.
- **No testimonials, no case studies, no named press, no advisor social handles** despite the personality-led pitch. The trust apparatus isn't built out.

### What we should consider taking from them

1. **The pricing page structure** — three tiers as a visible table, asset bands clearly stated, no gating. Probably the single best artifact in our catalog to mirror, with two changes: (a) we'd vary fees across tiers, not just deliverables; (b) we'd anchor the recommended tier visually.
2. **The named tech stack on the homepage.** Don't say "modern technology" — name Altruist or Schwab or Carson Group or whoever we end up custodying with; name Nitrogen if we're using Nitrogen; name the planning tool. Make the claim verifiable.
3. **The contrarian opening sentence pattern** — "We're the exact opposite of [the category default] (in a good way)" — adapted to our specific category opponent (which is probably "the 1.25% AUM all-things-to-all-people RIA," not the broker-dealer Derek is swinging at).
4. **The direct IAPD link in the footer.** Free credibility move.
5. **The capacity-cap mechanic** — even at multi-advisor scale, "we open N new client relationships per quarter" is a useful pre-qualification frame, though we'd want the number to be much larger.

### What we should deliberately *not* do

- **Don't list six segments.** Commit to two and own them with real surface area (free 401(k) tool, starter investor guide, etc.).
- **Don't build a surf-themed brand**, or any brand structurally dependent on a single founder's personal aesthetic. It is brittle to founder departure and to hiring.
- **Don't run a Wix-shaped site.** The captured CSS reveals heavy Wix typography overrides; the typo issue is a separate symptom of a small-firm build pipeline that doesn't have content review. We should invest in a proper design system.
- **Don't claim "evidence-based" without proof of method.** Intrepid asserts it; PWL demonstrates it (Ben Felix at 1M+ YouTube subscribers). If we make the claim, we need to demonstrate the discipline.
- **Don't ship a single-CTA contact form as the entire conversion path.** Modern lead capture needs progressive disclosure — start with the free tool, end with the call.
