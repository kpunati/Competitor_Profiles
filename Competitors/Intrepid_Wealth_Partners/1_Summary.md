# Intrepid Wealth Partners — Summary

> A solo-CFP boutique RIA (~$54M AUM, 1 advisor, 356 client accounts) on Florida's Atlantic coast led by founder Derek Notman — a "beach vibes" surf-themed brand targeting "high-earning millennials," doctors, founders, and executives, with a fully published three-tier fee schedule and a named modern tech stack (Altruist / Asset Map / Nitrogen).

## The headline

Intrepid Wealth Partners is a textbook **solo virtual-advisor** practice — one CFP doing the advising, one EA doing the back office, one contract compliance/operations person, and 356 client accounts on the Altruist platform. The brand work is loud and distinctive: surf vocabulary ("The Break / The Set / The Barrel" as pricing tiers, "Catch Your Wave" as CTA, "beach vibes" on the About page), an explicit "we're the exact opposite of when you hear the term 'financial advisor'" contrarian frame, and a founder origin story that doubles as personal-brand-building (Derek is also a "tech founder of Couplr AI"). What's genuinely interesting from our perspective is the **fee transparency**: every page links to a real published schedule (1.00% under $1M / 0.70% above / 0.55% above $3M), no minimums, "personality minimums" instead. They're low-threat to our two target segments — they aim at *already-successful* millennials, not first-time investors — but the pricing-page and tech-stack transparency are worth studying.

## What they sell, to whom

Discretionary fiduciary investment management — three named pricing tiers gated by investable-asset size (The Break <$100K, The Set $100K–$500K, The Barrel $500K+), but the **same asset-based fee schedule across all three** (1.00% under $1M, 0.70% above $1M, 0.55% above $3M, charged monthly). Lower tiers strip out custom-portfolio construction and tax-opportunity reviews; the AUM fee doesn't change with tier, only the deliverables do. Site explicitly names six target segments: "High-Earning Millennials, Doctors, Founders, Executives, Business Owners, Surfers & Mountain Climbers." Conversion is gated through a single contact form ("Help us get to know you") with the framing "We work with a select group of motivated people and only open 12 new client relationships per quarter."

The math: 356 accounts on $54M = ~$151K average account; that suggests a long tail of smaller The Break / The Set clients plus a handful of larger Barrel clients. A single advisor running 356 accounts is well past the conventional ~80-client solo ceiling, which suggests heavy reliance on Altruist + Asset Map automation rather than per-client deep planning.

## How big a threat

**Low.** Four-axis rubric: **Reach 1** (single solo advisor, ~$54M AUM, sparse blog with only 2 posts surfaced on the index, no podcast, no social presence captured), **Segment overlap 2** (explicitly targets millennials, but specifically the *already-high-earning* subset — doctors, founders, executives — which partially overlaps with our early-adult segment but misses the first-time-investor-setting-up-an-account moment we care about), **Momentum 1** (single-advisor capacity ceiling, no acquisitions/launches in source data, blog cadence light), **Capital backing 1** (founder-owned solo practice) → sum **5 → low.** They are not capacity-competitive with us and they aren't going to outspend a new entrant on content or product — but they have already validated *one specific positioning move* (named-segment millennial targeting with published fees and a personality-led brand) that we should pay attention to.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Full fee schedule on a public, indexed page.** No gating, no "schedule a call to learn pricing," no "fees vary by complexity." Pricing page literally lists the 1.00% / 0.70% / 0.55% bands. Competitive moat in a category where 80%+ of RIAs hide fees behind funnels.
- **Named tech stack on the homepage.** "Altruist. Asset Map. Nitrogen." Not asserted-but-not-shown — they name the actual SaaS products and Altruist is a client login link in the nav. Most RIAs in our catalog (Foundry, Walser, Streamline) claim "modern tech" but don't name it. This is what `modern_tech` should look like.
- **Six named target segments**, with the audacity to list "Surfers & Mountain Climbers" alongside Doctors and Executives. Builds a personality before it lists a benefit — exactly the kind of move that makes a 1-person firm feel hireable when scale-firm sites feel generic.
- **The contrarian opening line** — "We're the exact opposite of when you hear the term 'financial advisor' (in a good way)" — and "We don't have account minimums, but we do have personality minimums" — both clarify what the firm isn't, which is itself a positioning move. Compare to the standard "comprehensive fiduciary financial planning" mush most peer firms run as their hero.
- **Quarterly capacity cap is stated.** "Only open 12 new client relationships per quarter." Scarcity-pricing language done credibly (a single advisor *can't* take more), and it pre-qualifies the prospect on the form.
- **Founder origin doubles as content-leverage.** Derek is also "tech founder of Couplr AI" — the tech-founder story credentializes the named tech stack and the contrarian frame. The whole brand is internally consistent.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **Single-practitioner capacity ceiling.** 1 advisor for 356 client accounts is already at the high end of the solo-CFP envelope. Any meaningful growth means either hiring (which breaks the "personality minimums" pitch) or a service-quality decline. There is no succession plan visible.
- **Key-person risk is total.** Derek is the brand, the advisor, the content, and the tech-founder credentializing story. If he steps back, the firm is the EA and the contract compliance person.
- **Blog cadence is light.** Only two posts surfaced on the captured blog index (April 18, undated year — site uses month-day only). For a personality-led brand the publishing flywheel doesn't appear to be running.
- **Segment list is too long.** "High-earning millennials *and* doctors *and* founders *and* executives *and* business owners *and* surfers" is segment-specific in name but generalist in practice. Compare to Foundry's single ICP sentence — Intrepid's six-segment list dilutes any single segment's depth.
- **No 401(k) story at all.** No held-away-401(k) advice surface, no rollover funnel, no mid-career savings-rate content. Misses our 40-50 401(k) segment entirely.
- **No early-adult / first-time-investor surface.** The "millennial" framing is decorative — they want the millennial doctor or founder who already has assets, not the 26-year-old setting up a first Roth.
- **Site/regulatory copy discrepancy.** Footer claims "State Registered Investment Advisor" while IAPD shows a federal SEC file number. Either site copy is stale or there's an actual registration transition; either way it's a transparency wart on a firm whose pitch is transparency.

## What this means for our entry

Intrepid is **not a competitor we are taking prospects from**, but it is the firm in our catalog that best demonstrates the *form* of the position we want to occupy on a few axes:

1. **The published fee schedule** is what `transparent_pricing` should mean. Borrow the page structure (three tiers, asset-based bands shown as a table, no gating).
2. **The named tech stack** is what `modern_tech` should mean. Show the platforms, don't assert them.
3. **The contrarian opening frame** ("we're the opposite of when you hear 'financial advisor'") is what `contrarian_vs_conventional` should sound like in copy — earned, not generic.
4. **The capacity cap as scarcity language** is something a multi-advisor firm has to do differently (because we won't have a single-advisor ceiling), but the *mechanic* of pre-qualifying through "we open N relationships per quarter" is worth adapting.

What we should explicitly *not* copy: the six-segment list (we should commit to two — early-adults and mid-career 401(k) — and let everything else find us), the surf-themed branding (which is personal to Derek and brittle once anyone else joins the firm), and the founder-as-firm structural fragility.

The largest concrete entry opening **against Intrepid specifically** isn't a prospect dispute — it's the segments they ignore by listing too many: a focused entrant whose homepage says "first investment account at 27" or "your 401(k) on autopilot at 45" will out-position the six-segment "high-earning millennials / doctors / founders / executives / business owners / surfers" pile every single time.
