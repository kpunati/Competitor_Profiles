# Modera Wealth Management — Summary

> **Westwood NJ-headquartered, employee-owned fee-only fiduciary RIA** — founded **1983**, **$17.5B AUM, 238 employees, 106 advisors, 6,300+ clients**, 0 DRPs. The **largest NJ-headquartered RIA in this batch** and the geographic peer most relevant to USWM in Chatham. Employee-ownership is the defining differentiator vs. PE-backed Mather ($13.4B) and Mercer. Hero: *"Nothing makes us happier than helping you live well."*

## The headline

Modera is **USWM's biggest local benchmark** — same state (NJ), same regulatory environment, same fee-only fiduciary model. They've been doing this for 40+ years, run $17.5B AUM, and have grown via 10+ strategic acquisitions/expansions documented in their /our-story timeline (1983, 1995, 2011, 2013, 2014, 2015, 2016, more recent). The fact that they're **employee-owned** rather than PE-backed is genuinely unusual at their scale — most $10B+ RIAs in the U.S. are now PE-rolled or are themselves roll-ups.

For USWM, Modera is the **structural success benchmark**: this is what an independent, fee-only, employee-owned NJ RIA looks like at 40 years and $17B AUM. They didn't sell to a roll-up. They didn't take VC. They grew via deliberate strategic expansion. That's the long-run alternative to the Mercer/Mather acquisition path — and it's real.

Threat to USWM **as a competitor**: low. Modera targets HNW/UHNW families; doesn't compete for early-adult first-investors or 40-50 mid-career 401(k) holders specifically.

## Who they are

- **Founded**: **1983** (longest history in the catalog among large RIAs)
- **Size**: 238 employees, 106 advisors per Form ADV
- **HQ**: Westwood, NJ (~22 miles from USWM in Chatham)
- **Funding**: **Employee-owned** — defining differentiator at this scale
- **Regulatory**: SEC-registered (CRD 155649), 0 DRPs
- **AUM**: $17,678,214,162 as of 2026-06-17, serving 6,300+ clients
- **Client retention**: "11.2" stat on homepage (likely avg retention years — exceptional)

## What they sell, to whom

Two stated pillars: **Financial Planning · Investment Management.** ICP is HNW/UHNW families. Pricing not disclosed publicly. Six-page site IA (home, our-story, contact, blog, services + sub-pages).

**Positioning copy**:
- *"Nothing makes us happier than helping you live well"* (hero)
- *"Simplify your financial life... gain clarity, confidence, and peace of mind"*
- *"Sophisticated, fee-only wealth management in a highly personal way"*
- *"Relentless commitment to a client-first, fiduciary approach"*

## How big a threat

**Low.** Reach 3 ($17.5B + 40-year history + 6,300+ clients), Segment overlap 1 (HNW/UHNW — doesn't compete for USWM segments specifically), Momentum 2 (steady growth via acquisitions over decades; not a hot venture-funded firm), Capital backing 1 (employee-owned, no external dependency) → **7 → low** for USWM's segments specifically; would be **high** if competing for HNW families in Northern NJ.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Employee-ownership at $17.5B.** Genuinely unusual — most peers at this scale are PE-rolled. Modera's tagline could legitimately be "the alternative to selling to private equity."
- **40-year history visible in the brand.** Timeline page (1983 → present) treats firm history as marketing asset. Most launch-stage RIAs have nothing comparable; established firms underuse history. Modera leans in.
- **"Helping you live well" framing.** Outcome-focused (live well) vs. service-focused (planning + investing). Same hierarchy USWM should aim for — the work serves the life, not the other way around.
- **Strategic-growth timeline as transparency.** Lists when they acquired or expanded. Communicates deliberate growth rather than overnight scaling.
- **6,300+ clients.** Concrete number on the homepage. Beats "we serve high-net-worth families."

## Where they're vulnerable

- **No published pricing.** Same gap as most $10B+ RIAs.
- **"Broad target audience"** — homepage talks to a wide HNW audience; no named segments.
- **Comprehensive / holistic language present.** Hero is clean, but body copy uses some of the cliché territory USWM avoids.

## What this means for our entry

1. **Modera proves employee-ownership scales.** When USWM thinks about exit paths in 5-10 years, the choices aren't only "stay independent forever" vs. "sell to a roll-up." Modera is the model for *staying independent at scale* via employee-ownership. Worth keeping as the aspirational structure.
2. **History as a positioning asset.** Modera's 1983 founding is shown, dated, celebrated. USWM doesn't have 40 years yet — but the wirehouse departure story (Kean's 20+ years at Merrill/JPM/Citi/UBS/AIG → independent) is the equivalent narrative asset. Use it the way Modera uses 1983.
3. **Outcome framing over service framing.** Modera leads with "helping you live well." USWM leads with "Active investment management. Built around your life." Same shape — the outcome promise sits above the service description. Keep that hierarchy.
4. **Watch Modera's M&A activity.** They've been a serial acquirer of smaller NJ/regional RIAs for years. If USWM ever reaches $200M-$1B AUM, Modera is a probable acquirer (alongside Mather, Focus, Mercer). Monitor their public expansion announcements.

The narrowest single takeaway: **employee-ownership is a credible long-run alternative to the roll-up exit.** Modera proves it works at $17B; USWM should plan for it as the eventual structural model rather than assuming acquisition is the default endpoint.
