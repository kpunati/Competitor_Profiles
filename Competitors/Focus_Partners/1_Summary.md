# Focus Partners Wealth — Summary

> The consumer brand of national RIA aggregator Focus Financial Partners — **$181.86B AUM** across 220,585 client accounts, run by 1,025 advisors across 1,005 branches. 20 acquired boutique RIAs now legally consolidated under a single SEC-registered entity (CRD 159289).

## The headline

Focus Partners Wealth is what wealth management looks like at industrial scale: **$181.86 billion in regulatory AUM** (as of April 2026), 1,915 employees, 1,005 branches, 20 consolidated boutique brands under one SEC registration. Their site is polished, their voice is sophisticated, and their hero — "Others talk of a comprehensive approach. We embody it." — is one of the rare headlines in this category that has a competitor in mind. They are the category-defining example of *scale-plus-matching* as a positioning play. Two things to know that aren't visible on the site: they have at least one disciplinary disclosure on file with the SEC, and at least one named peer (Ritholtz Wealth) is positioning explicitly against their PE-backed model as a recruiting and marketing wedge.

## Who they are

- **Legal entity**: Focus Partners Wealth, LLC (CRD 159289 / SEC# 801-72862)
- **AUM**: **$181.86 billion** as of 2026-04-30 (ADV Item 5.F.2.c)
- **Client accounts**: 220,585 total (197,404 discretionary + 23,181 non-discretionary)
- **Founded**: parent Focus Financial Partners ~2006; the consolidated entity's own registration date needs ADV Part 1A
- **Size**: **1,915 employees** total / **1,025 advisory-function employees** / 1,005 branches
- **Where they're based**: 190 Carondelet Plaza, Suite 600, St. Louis, MO (notable — not NYC, despite being the parent's flagship consumer brand)
- **How they're funded**: industry context (verify before citing) — was NASDAQ: FOCS, taken private by Clayton, Dubilier & Rice in 2023
- **Brands under this single registration**: Atlas Private Wealth, The Colony Group, Buckingham Strategic Wealth, Churchill Management, GW & Wade, HoyleCohen, GYL Financial Synergies, Gratus Capital, NEIRG Wealth Management, and 11 others
- **Regulatory record**: at least one disciplinary disclosure on file with the SEC (severity needs ADV Part 2A pull)

## What they sell, to whom

A nine-pillar comprehensive financial planning service (net worth, investment, legacy, philanthropic, tax, education, retirement, health & insurance, life planning), aimed at affluent families across multiple life stages but predominantly mid-career and later. No pricing on the site; no self-serve signup; the buyer path is either "find a matched advisor" through their searchable directory or "Let's Chat" via a Calendly appointment or contact form. They sell relationship-led, advisor-mediated, multi-discipline wealth management at scale.

## How big a threat

**Medium.** They are not the firm we're competing with for a single client — they are the firm that *defines what the category looks like* at scale, with 1,005 branches and 20 consolidated brands. Their reach and polish are formidable. But their threat to a focused new entrant is bounded by three structural weaknesses: their messaging is uniform across life stages and doesn't visibly address early adults, their PE-backed ownership structure is something competitors (Ritholtz, others) actively position *against*, and they have a disciplinary disclosure on file in a category where trust is the product. They are exactly the kind of national incumbent a segmented, modern-tooling-forward entrant should expect to beat in specific niches.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Their hero is a positioning statement, not a tagline.** It names the category failure mode ("others talk of a comprehensive approach") and claims the fix ("we embody it"). This is rare in wealth management and worth imitating *structurally*.
- **Visual restraint as a luxury signal.** Monochrome palette, one accent color reserved only for CTAs, light-weight humanist sans typography (museo-sans at weight 300). They look expensive because they hold back.
- **FAQ blocks on every service-line page.** Good UX, also good SEO — they rank for question-shaped queries like "What is retirement planning?" Cheap pattern, high leverage.
- **Matched-advisor finder as the product.** The site funnels not to "contact sales" but to "find your specific advisor." For a firm with their scale, this turns a weakness (so many advisors!) into a feature (so many advisors that *one of them is right for you*).
- **Real-people photography, no stock illustration, no skylines.** Holds a higher standard than most of the category.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **Voice is the same across segments.** The retirement-planning hero sounds like the legacy-planning hero sounds like the wealth-management hero. Imagery segments; copy does not. A focused firm that writes meaningfully different language per segment wins prospects who feel directly addressed.
- **They do not visibly address early adults at all.** Every captured persona is mid-career or later. The 25–35 first-investment-account segment is open.
- **No fiduciary, fee-only, or independence claim on captured hero copy.** Whether that's by design or just missing on the surfaces we checked, the absence is exploitable by a firm that leads with those words.
- **No visible modern-tech story.** No client portal preview, no planning-software demo, no dashboard. For a prospect choosing between Focus and a robo-advisor, Focus has nothing concrete to show about what the day-to-day client experience actually looks like — only narrative.
- **Pricing entirely gated.** Industry default, but a weakness against a firm willing to be more transparent — especially for younger prospects.

## What this means for our entry

If our positioning is "the independent advisor who actually uses modern tools," Focus Partners Wealth gives us the clearest possible target. They are the category-defining example of *scale without modernity* and *comprehensiveness without segmentation*. The opening is to be the opposite on the two axes they're weakest on: visibly modern-tooling-forward (show the dashboard, show the planning software), and deliberately segmented per life stage (different hero, different copy, different imagery for early-adult vs. mid-career-401(k)). Don't try to out-comprehensive them — we cannot. Out-specific them, and out-modern them.
