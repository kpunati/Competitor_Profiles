# Brindle & Bay — Summary

> Frisco, TX retirement-focused fiduciary RIA founded by **Nick Davis (CFP®, ChFC®, RICP®)** — former full-time pastor for ten years in Texas before entering financial planning. **$184.8M AUM, 3 advisors, 4 employees** (per Form ADV), 0 DRPs. Single-segment ICP commitment to *people within 10 years of retirement*, executed through three named personas on the homepage: John & Laura ("Ready for Retirement"), Amy & Dave ("Work Optional"), Carolyn ("Single & Meaningful"). AUM-fee model around **"about 1% per year"** with a one-time planning setup cost on top; no tier table published.

## The headline

Brindle & Bay is a **pastor-turned-CFP retirement specialist** running a small, content-heavy practice in Dallas-Fort Worth. The Golden Rule is literally on the About page as the firm's operating principle ("We're stuck on the Golden Rule … Put yourself in other people's shoes"), which is a tonal differentiator most peer firms can't credibly claim — Nick's prior career as a pastor is the credibility anchor. The single-segment commitment is clean (retirement, 10-year horizon), the three named personas on the homepage are unusually specific for a small firm, and the content surface (blog · podcast · YouTube · "Nick's Weekly Memo" newsletter) is well-built. Threat to our retail RIA is **low** — same boundary case as [The_Peak_Fp](../The_Peak_Fp/1_Summary.md): retirement-focused, doesn't compete for our segments — but Brindle & Bay shows what a *Texas* retirement-RIA looks like at 2x The Peak FP's AUM with slightly different positioning leverage.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Three named ICP personas on the homepage.** The "John & Laura / Amy & Dave / Carolyn" approach is meaningfully more vivid than abstract segment descriptions. Each persona names: their relationship status, their retirement timeline, their specific concerns, their specific priorities. This is what segment-specific marketing should look like at the *individual landing-page* level — Brindle & Bay puts all three personas on the homepage, but the technique scales naturally to three separate landing pages too.
- **Pastor-turned-CFP origin story** as the irreducible trust anchor. Most RIAs differentiate on credentials or technology; Nick differentiates on *prior-career empathy*. "I grew up in a modest midwestern home and was influenced by my grandfather. He was a bricklayer who retired at 61 and enjoyed many years of life after work." Story does more positioning work than any tagline.
- **Golden Rule as the operating principle.** "Put yourself in other people's shoes. Treat them as you would treat yourself." Risky framing for a CFP firm (sounds saccharine in isolation), but earns credibility through Nick's pastor background. Works because the source is authentic.
- **Honest pricing disclosure even without a tier table.** The FAQ literally says "Most people find this to be about 1% per year" — which is more specific than 90% of the gated-pricing RIAs in our catalog. Combined with the one-time setup cost disclosed (variable, discussed up front), it's more transparent than Foundry Financial despite not publishing a table.
- **"Nick's Weekly Memo" newsletter as the founder-personality content surface**. "This isn't market commentary, it's Nick's thoughts to help you make sense of what's going on in the financial world." Smart framing — positions the newsletter as *interpretation* rather than *prediction*, which is the right register for a fiduciary.
- **Form CRS link visible in footer.** Walking the regulatory-transparency talk.
- **Investment Committee model** (3 outside CPAs) is interesting at small scale — separates investment-policy decision-making from day-to-day client relationship work, gives clients confidence that allocation decisions are not made by a single founder. Smart governance for a small firm.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **No published AUM tier table.** "About 1%" without a band loses the prospect at the comparison-shopping stage when they're looking at firms that do publish (Peak FP, Intrepid Wealth Partners). Could be a 5-minute fix.
- **No one-time / hourly option.** Explicit "we don't offer hourly." A prospect with $200K and a single retirement question can't enter the funnel. Filters out lower-value prospects (probably intentional) but also filters out *future high-value prospects who first interact via a low-stakes engagement.*
- **No early-adult / 401(k) surface.** Same as all retirement-RIAs in our catalog.
- **Family-business framing without succession plan disclosed.** Nick + Connie (presumably spouse) + Tori + Andrew = a 4-named-employee team where 2 share a surname. If Nick and Connie both step back, the firm becomes Tori + Andrew. Not necessarily a problem but worth tracking.
- **Mid-tier content cadence.** Podcast at Episode 31 is real cadence but not at PWL or Streamline Planning scale; YouTube channel referenced but no captured subscriber count.
- **Geographic anchor is single Frisco TX office.** National-virtual delivery is implied but not emphasized.

## What this means for our entry

Brindle & Bay is **not on our threat radar**. Strategic value:

1. **Three named ICP personas on the homepage** is a borrowable pattern. For our two target segments, the equivalent would be persona triples per segment — "Alex, 26, first investment account / Maya, 31, post-grad-school side income → Roth optimization / Jordan, 28, just-married first joint plan" for early-adults; and a parallel triple for mid-career 401(k). Persona names + relationships + concerns + priorities — concrete enough to feel hireable.
2. **Founder origin story as positioning** works when the prior-career credibility is real. Brindle & Bay's pastor-to-CFP arc is the right shape: a *former vocation* gives credibility to the *current vocation's promise* (in this case, that the financial advisor will treat people pastorally). If anyone on our founding team has a prior career that maps to our value promise (e.g. data scientist → "we built the modeling our advice runs on"; teacher → "we'll actually educate, not pitch"; engineer → "we'll fix systems, not sell products"), lead with it.
3. **Investment Committee model at small scale** is a governance pattern worth borrowing. Even for a 2–3-advisor launch firm, having an outside Investment Committee of 2–3 named CPAs / CFAs separates allocation-decision-making from client-relationship work, and gives the prospect confidence that their portfolio isn't built by one human.
4. **The 1% "most people find this to be" framing** is a useful middle ground between "gated entirely" and "fully published tier table." If our pricing isn't ready to publish in tiers at launch, this language is better than no disclosure.

What we should explicitly *not* take: the no-hourly / no-one-time policy (we *want* the lower-stakes entry product to capture early-adult prospects), the Golden-Rule framing without an authenticating personal story (saccharine in isolation), and the single-geography anchor (we should plan national from day one).

The narrowest single takeaway: **three named ICP personas on the homepage is the cheapest, highest-impact positioning move we can make.** Brindle & Bay puts three on one page; the more disciplined version is three per segment, on a dedicated segment landing page each.
