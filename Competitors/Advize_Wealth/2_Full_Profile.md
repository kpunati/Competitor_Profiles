# Advize Wealth Management — Full Profile

*This is the deep dive. For a quick read, see `1_Summary.md`.*

## The big picture

Advize Wealth Management is a one-person, fee-only RIA in Rochester, Michigan, built around its founder Colin Overweg, CFP®. The firm's pitch is right there in the meta description: *"an independent, fee-only financial planning firm powered by the industries leading technology."* Colin's origin story — *"I was sick of commissions, proprietary products, sales quotes, clucky technology and being told how to help my clients so left the broker dealer world forever and launched Advize Wealth!"* — is the entire firm in one sentence. He left a broker-dealer, planted a flag on independence and fee-only, and pointed the website at busy professionals who want planning advice without product pushers.

Operationally, this is a solo-advisor practice with a Wix-built website, a Mailchimp newsletter ("Colin's Column"), a Zoom-scheduled intro funnel, and a YouTube/Instagram content engine. The SEC IAPD lists the firm as ADVIZE WEALTH MANAGEMENT (CRD 297380), an ACTIVE single-branch RIA registered as Advize Wealth Management PLLC with no disciplinary disclosures. There is no AUM in our captured data — the manual ADV pull is still pending.

For us — a pre-launch independent RIA targeting early adults and 40–50-year-old 401(k) holders with experienced-advisor-plus-modern-tooling positioning — Advize is interesting *not* as a head-on competitor but as the closest single-person version of the exact thesis we're testing. Colin claims the same two halves we want to claim ("independent" + "modern tech"). He's been in market long enough to have a credentials stack. And he's tiny. The question for us isn't "can we beat Advize?" — it's "is the gap he leaves *because* he's solo + content-led + Wix-built the opening we should aim for?"

## Who they sell to

The About page is unambiguous about the named ICP: *"Advize Wealth Management is a comprehensive financial planning firm specialized in helping busy professionals achieve financial freedom."* And the StartHere page narrows it further with its H2: *"READY TO RETIRE ON YOUR TERMS?!"*

So the customer is **busy mid-career-and-later professionals planning their way to retirement**. Three signals worth pairing:

- **About-page copy**: "*helping busy professionals achieve financial freedom*" — the word "busy" is doing real work here; it implies time-poor, high-earning, delegation-ready clients.
- **StartHere hero**: "*Ready to retire on your terms?!*" — frames the relationship around retirement readiness, not accumulation, college planning, or first investments.
- **Founder bio asides**: Colin mentions guest lecturing at universities, but there is *no* student/early-adult landing experience. The visible funnel is built for someone who already has assets to plan with.

The unspoken claim is "smart, fee-only, no-product-pitch planning for established earners who want a real advisor not a salesperson." There is no segmentation between, say, a 35-year-old tech worker and a 55-year-old executive; the same single funnel serves both. The early-adult segment is not addressed at all.

## What they sell

A single, packaged service: **comprehensive financial planning, fee-only, advice-only**. The defining claim from the About page is *"No products, just advice!"* — meaning no commissioned mutual funds, no annuities, no insurance pushed for kickbacks. The fee model is not visible on the site (you have to watch a video on the StartHere page to see it), but the structure implied by "fee-only" and "no products" is most likely a flat planning fee, an AUM percentage, or a subscription.

The packaging is described abstractly rather than listed as tiers. There is no "Bronze / Silver / Gold," no separate "investment management" vs. "planning" line item, no service-pillar grid. The closest thing to a product description is the two-step pitch on the StartHere page:

1. **Watch my fees & services video** (a YouTube embed)
2. **Schedule a free intro zoom Meeting** → links to `scheduler.zoom.us/advize/intro`

How a buyer gets started: watch a video, book a Zoom, talk to Colin. There is no self-serve signup, no questionnaire, no instant quote. The entire conversion path is "let me explain it to you on camera, then on a call."

The captured site does not contain a `/services` or `/pricing` page in the main nav. There is a "More" dropdown in the header that the page-discovery heuristic did not crawl — fee details and any service-tier breakdown may be hiding behind there. Worth a manual revisit.

- **Imagery**: a single hero photograph of a modern office interior, desaturated, grey-toned (homepage). The hollow-cyan-outline button is loud and feels more like a SaaS product page than a wealth firm; it's the single most "modern tech" visual choice on the site, and it's working overtime to carry the brand's "modern" claim.

## How they sound

The voice is **a single person talking on camera, lightly transcribed.** First person, exclamation-heavy, emoji-friendly, conversational to the point of looseness. There are visible typos ("clucky" instead of "clunky"; "have been feature in multiple publications" missing a word). Most of the body copy is on the About page, where Colin writes the bio himself; the rest of the site is sparse and uses the same first-person voice in micro-doses. This is the polar opposite of Focus Partners' editorial composure. Where Focus reads like a magazine, Advize reads like a YouTube channel description.

- **Tone**: warm, casual, founder-led. Earnest more than polished. Light humor.
- **Reading level**: roughly grade 7–8 — short clauses, plain words, exclamations.

- **Pronouns**: predominantly **I** — *"Hi, I'm Colin"*, *"my passion"*, *"I live in Michigan with my amazing wife and daughter"* — with occasional **we** (*"we believe"*) that mostly means "I." The pronoun pattern makes the firm-is-one-person reality unhidden.

## The story they tell

Advize's positioning narrative is **"the fee-only solo advisor who escaped the broker-dealer world and uses modern tech instead of pushing products."** The story is built on a clear villain (the broker-dealer that wanted Colin to push commissioned products with clunky tools) and a clear hero (Colin, who left to start his own thing). The founding moment is told as a personal liberation: *"left the broker dealer world forever."* This is a textbook independent-RIA founding story, told more candidly than most.

The implicit competitor Advize is swinging at is the **commission-driven, captive-broker advisor** — Edward Jones, the wirehouse rep, the bank-owned planner whose recommendations follow product economics rather than client interests. The *"No products, just advice!"* tagline is a direct rebuttal of that competitor type. Notably, Advize is *not* swinging at the robo-advisors or the national RIA aggregators; their fight is upstream of the modern fintech debate, and downstream of the broker-dealer one.

The worldview being offered: *you deserve a real, fiduciary-style human advisor who is incentivized only by your retainer — not a salesperson dressed as one. And you can have that without sacrificing the modern tools you'd expect from a fintech.* The "modern" claim is doing two things at once: differentiating from the broker-dealer past (stuck in clunky software), and reassuring against the fintech present (you don't lose the digital experience by hiring a human).

That positioning is precisely the same one our thesis claims for us — *independent + modern tooling*. The difference is that Advize claims it as a one-person Wix-built firm; we plan to claim it with a multi-person team and meaningful in-house tooling. The audit question is whether Colin's version is credible enough to crowd us out in the segments where we overlap.

## What they promise

The hierarchy of promises a visitor sees:

1. **Primary** (the headline above the fold, desktop): *"MODERN FINANCIAL PLANNING"* — three words, no verb, no specific promise. On mobile, the supporting line surfaces: *"Money Touches Every Part of Your Life / We Help You Use It to Accomplish Your Dreams."* This is the de facto value-prop line, hidden from desktop visitors by the lazy-load behavior.
2. **Secondary** (the meta description that shows in search results): *"Advize Wealth Management is an independent, fee-only financial planning firm powered by the industries leading technology."* — three concrete claims in one sentence: independent, fee-only, tech-powered. This is the most informative line on the entire surface.
3. **Tertiary** (About page positioning lines): *"comprehensive financial planning firm specialized in helping busy professionals achieve financial freedom"* and *"No products, just advice!"* — narrows the ICP (busy professionals), states the outcome (financial freedom), and reinforces the no-commissions claim.

The notable mismatch: the homepage hero is the *least* specific surface (three abstract words), while the meta description is the *most* specific. A first-time visitor sees only "MODERN FINANCIAL PLANNING" and has to click "ABOUT" to learn anything substantive. The promise hierarchy is structurally inverted from what most wealth firms do.

## How they ask for the sale

The call-to-action strategy:

- **Primary CTA**: **"START HERE"** — appears in the top-right of every page as a persistent cyan-outlined button, *and* as a big in-hero CTA on mobile. Clicking routes to `/starthere`, which itself is a two-step funnel: (1) watch a video, (2) "SCHEDULE HERE" which links to `scheduler.zoom.us/advize/intro`.
- **Secondary CTA**: **"Schedule"** — solid blue button on the Contact page, also routing to Zoom.
- **Tertiary CTA**: **"Subscribe to COLIN'S COLUMN"** — newsletter signup in the footer of every page, routing to a Mailchimp signup (`eepurl.com/gjy36j`).
- **Friction level**: medium-low. There is no self-serve signup or instant quote, but the path from website to booked call is unusually short for the category: hero CTA → 2-step page → Zoom scheduler. No contact form gating, no "we'll be in touch within 48 hours," no human triage between you and Colin's calendar.
- **What this tells us about their sales motion**: founder-led, video-mediated, single-funnel. Colin is both the marketing channel and the closer. The "watch the video before the call" pattern is a deliberate qualifier — it filters out prospects who won't sit through 5–10 minutes of education, and it gets fee/process objections out of the way before the call so the call itself can be relationship-building. This is a sophisticated solo-advisor playbook borrowed from creator-economy practice.

For our early-adult segment, even Advize's "watch a video to learn the fee" requires more commitment than a sticker price would; for our 40–50 401(k) segment, this is probably acceptable.

## What they believe

The worldview underneath everything else: **financial advice should be unbundled from product sales, delivered by a human, and built on a real relationship — and the technology stack should serve that, not replace it.** Two related convictions:

1. **The broker-dealer model is corrupt by structure, not by individual bad actors.** Colin's founding story isn't "I had a bad boss." It's "the system makes you push products you don't believe in, run on tools you can't use, with quotas that override fiduciary judgment — so the only escape is to leave entirely." This is a worldview, not a job change.
2. **The right scale of a wealth practice is the scale at which one human can know all their clients.** There is no acquisition strategy, no junior advisor bench, no "as we grow we will..." There is also no team page (because there is no team). The firm is built to be a one-person practice; the marketing engine (newsletter, YouTube, guest lectures) is built to extend Colin's reach without scaling the practice itself. This is a different bet from both the national-aggregator side (Focus) and the multi-advisor independent-RIA side (where we sit).

A third belief, visible in the visual identity and content cadence: **content marketing is the firm.** The blog, the videos, the newsletter, the media mentions — these aren't lead-gen support for the practice, they *are* the practice's public face. Colin's personal media presence is the primary asset, and the RIA underneath it is the monetization layer.

## What this means for our entry

### What's working for them

- **A clear, named villain in the founding story.** *"I was sick of commissions, proprietary products, sales quotes, clucky technology..."* is specific, vivid, and gives the reader something to be against. Most independent RIAs say "fee-only" but never describe what they're escaping. Colin describes it in detail. Result: his *"no products, just advice"* line lands with weight that a generic "we're fee-only" tagline never could.
- **"Powered by the industries leading technology" as a tagline-level claim.** Whether or not the underlying tech is actually best-in-class (the site itself is a Wix template, so the claim is at minimum unsubstantiated on the surface), making "modern tech" a top-three claim alongside "independent" and "fee-only" is exactly the positioning move our thesis is built on. He's demonstrated the claim *is* marketable as part of an independent-RIA pitch.
- **The StartHere two-step funnel.** Watch-a-video-then-book-a-call qualifies prospects, surfaces fees before the conversation, and reduces no-show rates. Borrowed from the creator economy and unusually well-deployed in wealth management. We should consider this for at least one of our segments.
- **First-person founder voice.** *"Hi, I'm Colin."* The category is full of "we" voices that hide behind corporate distance. A confident first-person voice is rare and trust-building for prospects who want to know who they're actually talking to.
- **CFP® + FPA Board + Investopedia Top 100 + media presence as a credentials stack.** Each individually is common; stacked together they form a credible expert-authority signal that punches above his AUM weight. Earned-media-as-credentials is a real strategy.
- **Newsletter as the relationship hook.** "Subscribe to Colin's Column" appears on every page footer. He's been compounding an email list for years — that asset is more durable than any individual blog post.

### Where they're vulnerable

- **The "modern technology" claim is asserted, not shown.** A Wix-built marketing site with no client-portal preview, no planning-software screenshots, no dashboard demo is a thin substrate to build a "powered by the industries leading technology" claim on. A prospect doing a careful comparison would notice — Wix is a 2000s website builder, not a modern tech statement. **This is the single largest opening for us:** show the actual planning tooling on the marketing site (RightCapital, eMoney, screenshot the client portal, show a real Roth conversion calculator) and the same positioning Colin claims, we can claim more credibly.
- **Solo-advisor capacity ceiling.** One person can hold roughly 50–80 client relationships well. Beyond that, service quality degrades. A growing prospect base will outrun Colin's calendar. A multi-advisor firm with role specialization (planner + investment lead + tech ops) can grow past that ceiling without sacrificing relationship quality.
- **No early-adult content surface at all.** The hero, the About copy, the StartHere page — every visible surface is aimed at "busy professionals" who already have assets to plan with and are thinking about retirement. A 28-year-old opening their first taxable brokerage account will feel addressed nowhere on this site. The early-adult segment is a clean miss.
- **Voice fragility — typos, run-ons, sparse desktop content.** "Clucky technology" appears twice in copy. The About-page sentence *"have been feature in multiple publications"* is missing a word. Desktop homepage below-the-fold is essentially empty. A prospect who reads carefully will notice. A firm presenting itself as a real organization (rather than a single person's personal brand) cannot get away with this.
- **No fiduciary statement on captured pages.** Fee-only implies fiduciary, but a prospect doing due diligence will search for the word. Its absence is a missed trust signal.
- **No testimonials.** Whether by compliance choice or oversight, the SEC's 2021 marketing rule changes permit testimonials with proper disclosure. Using them well is a competitive advantage Advize is not capturing.
- **No social proof beyond Colin himself.** No team page (because there's no team), no client roster (privacy-appropriate), no client-outcome stories. The trust signal is "trust Colin." For prospects who don't already know Colin from his content, this is a smaller surface than a multi-person firm can offer.
- **Geographic concentration.** Rochester, MI is a small market. Colin's virtual practice can reach nationally, but the in-person dimension is constrained.
- **Wix as a tech stack is a tell.** Site is hosted on `static.wixstatic.com` with a `<meta name="generator" content="Wix.com Website Builder">` declaration. A multi-advisor firm with even a modest dev investment can have a substantially better digital footprint, which lends actual evidence to "modern."

### How well do they serve each of our target segments?

- **Early adults setting up proper investment accounts**: **Weak / Not aimed at this segment.** The ICP is named as "busy professionals achieving financial freedom" — implies someone with income, savings, and a retirement horizon already in mind. The StartHere page leads with *"Ready to retire on your terms?!"* — an explicit retirement frame. There is no content, no imagery, no funnel step for someone opening their first Roth IRA or trying to decide between Vanguard and Fidelity. **Opening for us: this segment is unaddressed by Advize and underaddressed by the category overall — clean entry point.**
- **40–50 year olds focused on 401(k) management**: **OK.** Advize's stated ICP — "busy professionals achieving financial freedom" — overlaps directly with this segment, and Colin's content engine (videos titled around retirement risk, healthcare costs, safety in retirement) speaks to retirement-anxious mid-career prospects. But there is no 401(k)-specific surface: no rollover content captured, no "I just left a job and have a 401(k) sitting" funnel, no held-away-401(k) management offering visible. **The segment is targeted thematically but not operationally.** A firm with a dedicated 401(k)-management-and-rollover landing page and a planner who specializes in employer-plan coordination would outpoint Advize on segment fit.

### What we should consider taking from them

- **The founding-villain narrative.** Write our origin story with a specific named villain (broker-dealer commissions, robo-advisor cold detachment, generic "wealth management" that doesn't know which life stage you're in — pick one and dramatize it). Generic origin stories don't work; named ones do.
- **The "watch a video before the call" funnel.** Adopt this for at least one segment — probably the early-adult segment, where a 5-minute "how we work and what it costs" video filters serious prospects from tire-kickers far better than a contact form does.
- **First-person founder voice for the founder, third-person for the firm.** Where we have an individual advisor talking, let them sound like a person ("Hi, I'm X"). Where we describe the firm as an entity, use "we." Don't mush the two voices.
- **A credentials stack, not a single credential.** CFP® alone is table stakes; CFP® + FPA board + named press + university lectures + named awards is a credibility *stack*. Build the stack visibly on the About page.
- **A named newsletter with personality.** "Colin's Column" is a better newsletter brand than "Advize Newsletter" would be. Give our newsletter a name with character.
- **Specificity in the anti-positioning.** *"No products, just advice!"* is two-clause specific. Our equivalent should be similarly compressed.

### What we should deliberately *not* do

- **Build the site on Wix.** It contradicts the "modern tech" claim every time a prospect inspects the page source or compares the experience to a real product. We should look like a software company that does wealth management, not a wealth manager who bought a template.
- **Hide pricing entirely.** The watch-a-video-to-see-fees move is a clever compromise, but it still gates the most basic question. For our early-adult segment we should publish at least a starting price; for the 401(k) segment we can use a tier-with-ranges approach.
- **Run all segments through one funnel.** "Busy professionals" is the lowest-common-denominator ICP that hides the segments under it. Our site should branch on segment from the homepage.
- **Ship with typos and run-on sentences.** Colin can get away with this because his voice is "the human, not the firm." A firm presenting itself as a real organization cannot.
- **Lean on a single founder's media presence as the firm's primary marketing asset.** Long-term that's a key-person risk. We should distribute the content engine across multiple advisors from the start.
- **Skip the fiduciary statement.** Fee-only implies it; saying it explicitly is free. Put "fiduciary" on the homepage.
