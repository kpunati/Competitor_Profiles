# Kitces.com — Full Profile

*This is the deep dive. For a quick read, see `1_Summary.md`.*

## The big picture

Kitces.com is not a wealth-management firm in the sense of every other competitor in this set. It is the personal media, research, and education platform of Michael Kitces — arguably the most influential single voice in US financial planning over the past fifteen years — operating under the "Nerd's Eye View" brand. The homepage tagline is unambiguous about who it talks to: *"Financial planning knowledge to help you better serve your clients. Practice management ideas you can implement yourself."* This is a B2B publication for financial advisors, not a marketing site for retail clients.

What gets sold here is access to a research/education stack: free blog content (Nerd's Eye View), paid subscription research (The Kitces Report — published reports on AdvisorTech, FP Productivity, Marketing, Wellbeing), CE-credit-eligible courses for CFP/IMCA/IAR continuing education, live events (Kitces Marketing Summit, IAR Ethics CE Day, Tax Intensive CE Day), the Financial Advisor Success podcast, and Michael's affiliated speaking and consulting work. There is a tiny "FOR CONSUMERS" entry in the navigation that points to a single page intended to refer retail clients out to advisors — not to acquire them.

For us — a pre-launch independent RIA running a market-landscape analysis — Kitces does not threaten prospect acquisition. The reason he is in this profile set at all is that he shapes the *category narrative* every other RIA in our competitive set is reading: how peer firms think about fees, services, niching, marketing funnels, succession, AdvisorTech adoption, and the post-AI value of human planning are all heavily downstream of what gets published on this site. The 78,507 advisors he says read the newsletter are our peers, and many of them will recognize a frame we used because they read it in a Kitces Report first. The right posture toward Kitces is to be a well-read consumer of his work, not a debater of it.

## Who they sell to

This is the section that diverges hardest from the rest of our competitor set. Kitces sells to **financial advisors, advisor firms, and adjacent industry roles** — not to retail wealth-management clients. The site's own primary-navigation taxonomy makes this explicit. The top-level menu reads:

- **FOR ADVICERS** (the home category — includes Live Events, Training Courses, Marketing Summit, Financial Planning Value Summit, IAR Ethics CE Day, Tax Intensive CE Day, Directors of Financial Planning, Business Solutions, Advicer Manifesto)
- **FOR CONSUMERS** (two sub-items only: "Gen X/Y" and "Retirees" — both pointing to the same `/financial-planning-solutions-for-consumers/` URL, i.e. a single referral-out page)
- **ENGAGE** (Speaking, Consulting — Michael's personal services to firms and event organizers)
- **RESEARCH** (AdvisorTech, FP Productivity, Marketing, Wellbeing, Research Spotlight, Data Exports — The Kitces Report subscription)
- **BECOME MEMBER** (Individual, Group, CE Catalog, IAR CE FAQs)

The asymmetry between "FOR ADVICERS" (8 sub-items, deep navigation) and "FOR CONSUMERS" (2 cosmetic items both linking to one page) is the entire customer-segment story in a single design decision.

Within the advisor audience there are several sub-segments visible from the content and the events:

- **Solo and small-firm RIA practitioners** — the heaviest readership, served by practice-management posts, marketing posts, and the Marketing Summit
- **CFP / IMCA / CPA / IAR designation holders looking for CE credit** — served by paid CE courses and CE-eligible reading
- **Advisor-firm Directors of Financial Planning** — a dedicated community page for this role
- **AdvisorTech vendors and analysts** — served by the AdvisorTech research report, the monthly "Latest in AdvisorTech" column, and the AdvisorTech Solutions Map
- **Academic/industry researchers** — the Kitces Research arm itself runs studies (currently the 2026 Marketing study) that other industry analysts cite

What's notable about the imagery and tone: there is no real customer photography in the traditional wealth-firm sense. The site uses a single banner photo of Michael Kitces speaking, plus stock-ish illustrations on report covers. This is consistent with the actual customer being an industry insider who already knows who Michael is — the work does not need a photo of "you, the customer" because the customer is buying access to Michael's research, not a representation of themselves.

**Against our target segments (early-adult investors and 40–50 year old 401(k)-focused mid-career)**: Kitces does not address these segments at the prospect level. He addresses *the advisors who serve them.* The Gen X/Y referral page exists but is a one-page courtesy, not an acquisition surface. We will never compete with him for a retail client.

## What they sell

A multi-product stack, all monetized in different ways. From the captured navigation:

- **Nerd's Eye View blog** — free. The flagship product, ~5 posts/week, full-text RSS, named bylines. Acquisition funnel for everything else.
- **Newsletter / Blog Updates email** — free. Sign-up CTA reads *"Join 78,507 fellow Financial Advicers, get our latest research as it's released, and receive a free copy of The Kitces Report on 'Quantifying the Value of Financial Planning Advice'."* The free report is the lead magnet.
- **Membership / The Kitces Report (paid subscription)** — Individual and Group tiers. Gives access to in-depth research reports, CE quizzes against blog content, and the four research verticals (AdvisorTech, FP Productivity, Marketing, Wellbeing). Pricing is gated — not visible on the captured homepage; sits behind the "BECOME MEMBER" funnel. The "Please contact your Firm's Group Admin" H3 visible on the homepage is a logged-out group-subscription paywall message — confirming a B2B group-subscription channel sold to firms.
- **CE Catalog / Online CE Courses** — CFP CE, IMCA CE, CPE, IAR CE-eligible coursework. The most concrete new product visible at capture: the "Plan Presentation Meetings course."
- **Live events** — Kitces Marketing Summit (June 4, 2026), IAR Ethics CE Day, Tax Intensive CE Day, Financial Planning Value Summit. Most are virtual; some in-person.
- **Podcasts** — *Financial Advisor Success* (488 episodes, hour-plus interviews with successful advisors), *Kitces & Carl* (shorter conversation format with Carl Richards). Both free, ad-supported / brand-supported.
- **Research data exports / AdvisorTech Map** — *"FinTechMap Purchase Updates Export"* in nav suggests sale of structured industry data to vendors and consultants.
- **Speaking** — Michael speaks at industry events (visible "Out and About" widget: May 12 Wealthbox webinar, May 21 LaSalle St. Securities webinar, May 26 FPA NorCal in San Francisco).
- **Consulting** — Michael's personal consulting work for firms and vendors.
- **Submit-your-content channels** — Submit Podcast Guest, Submit Guest Webinar, Submit Guest Post, Submit Summit Guest Presentation. The site monetizes attention by *accepting* contributed content as well as producing original, which means a meaningful share of bylines and podcast guests are advisors marketing themselves through Kitces. This is structurally important: the platform is partly an earned-media channel for ambitious advisors.

How a buyer gets started: the marketing funnel is clearly **email-list-first, paywall-second**. Free blog → free newsletter (with free lead-magnet report) → paid Kitces Report subscription / paid courses / paid events. The primary on-page CTA across the homepage is *"GET BLOG UPDATES!"* — i.e. capture the email before anything else.

## How they look

The first three seconds: dated, dense, deeply functional. A deep navy header (`rgb(0, 84, 143)`) with a personal portrait of Michael in a slightly-illustrated cartoon avatar style in the top-left corner. The hero is a flat navy block with the tagline in plain white text and a green button. Below the hero the page becomes a long single-column blog feed with right-side ad/promo modules. The feel is *industry trade publication*, not modern web brand — more *FinancialPlanning.com* than *Stripe.com*. It does not look like the rest of our competitor set, and it does not try to.

- **Colors**: dominant body navy `rgb(0, 84, 143)` and a deeper navy `rgb(0, 42, 74)`, a brighter mid-blue accent `rgb(86, 163, 217)`, plus a distinctive olive-green CTA `rgb(128, 163, 64)` / `rgb(130, 165, 2)`. The palette is busy by 2026 standards — three blues and a green — and the green CTA color is uncommon in financial services (most use blue or orange). It reads "earnest, slightly old-school, not designed-by-an-agency."
- **Type**: `Lato, sans-serif` for both headings and body, weight 400 throughout. Body 18px, paragraph 18px. Lato is a free, ubiquitous Google font — competent, neutral, not distinctive. The all-400-weight choice gives headings less visual hierarchy than most modern brands; the long-form blog feed reads as a wall of text on first scroll.
- **Imagery**: a single hero portrait of Michael Kitces speaking (mid-gesture, brown blazer, in front of a blurred audience background — a stage shot, not a studio portrait). Otherwise the page is text + report-cover thumbnails + small partner-logo strip. There is no client/customer photography, no stock illustrations of families or skylines. Iconography is minimal. The visual story is *the words and the man*, not *the lifestyle*.
- **Layout**: dense and long. Document/viewport at 1440px desktop. The full homepage screenshot is one of the tallest in our set — primary content is a chronological blog feed of ~5 recent posts with full lead-paragraph previews and "READ MORE..." links, plus right-rail widgets (Popular Posts, What Michael's Reading, Out And About event schedule). Strong "this is a 2010s WordPress blog that grew up" energy.
- **Logo**: bracket-K mark followed by "KITCES" wordmark in a navy-on-navy header. Stacked next to Michael's small cartoon avatar. Personal brand foreground; institutional brand secondary.
- **Primary button**: olive green `rgb(128, 163, 64)` background, white text, 3px border-radius, 4×8 padding. Tight padding, near-square corners. The button is small relative to most 2026 web buttons — sized for an information-dense layout, not for a marketing landing page.

The visual identity is not where Kitces competes. The credibility is in the writing and the byline, and the design is deliberately (or at least durably) subordinate. That is its own design choice — a publication that redesigned to look like Stripe would lose some of its earned trade-press authority. The audience knows what they came for.

## How they sound

The voice is dense, qualified, professorial, and unmistakably one person's. Michael Kitces writes like an academic who has read every research paper in the field, is slightly impatient with people who haven't, and is doing his best to be helpful anyway. Sentences are long, clauses are stacked, and parentheticals are constant — he is the rare web writer who uses *em-dashes, parentheticals, and "Which suggests..."* opener phrases as structural tools, not as ornaments. Posts are 1,200–3,000+ words. The byline on every flagship post is the named author, never "the editors" or "the team."

- **Tone**: research-led, analytic, gently opinionated. Reads like the methodology section of a paper that has decided to be helpful.
- **Reading level**: comfortably college-level, sometimes graduate. Heavy on industry jargon (`Monte Carlo`, `NUA`, `RSU`, `Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities`, `marginal vs effective tax rate`, `advicer`) that he assumes the reader already knows.
- **Sentence style**: long and considered. Stacked clauses. Frequent "Notably, the study also found…" / "Which suggests…" / "Yet the reality is…" connectives. Pivots from observation to implication and back inside a single sentence.
- **Words they own**: ***Advicer*** (Kitces's deliberate spelling, used as the term for the audience — *"financial advicers,"* *"FOR ADVICERS,"* *"Advicer Manifesto"*) — this is the single most distinctive verbal choice on the site and a positioning statement in one word. Also: *Nerd's Eye View, AdvisorTech, FinTechMap, finfluencers, mailbag, Weekend Reading, FA Success.* The Kitces Research framing — *"Kitces Research on Advisor Marketing finds that…"* — is a deliberate format used to brand findings as theirs.
- **Words they avoid**: marketing-deck words. "Empower," "elevate," "world-class," "comprehensive," "trusted partner" — absent. He does not write in marketing-firm voice; he writes in trade-journal voice.
- **Pronouns**: *I* (Michael) and *we* (the editorial team / "we hope you enjoy this episode") and *you* (the advisor reader). Notably, *you the consumer* essentially never appears on the home page.

**A sample, in their words:**

> "Marketing for new clients through education has long been a popular approach for financial advisors. […] As a result, Kitces Research on Advisor Marketing finds that education-based marketing is amongst the most popular marketing tactics that advisors pursue, behind only referrals from clients and centers of influence, and networking (in-person or online). Yet the reality is that nearly half of advisors fail to get a single new client in a full year of trying their education-based marketing approach."

That paragraph in a nutshell: an industry observation → a Kitces Research citation → a pointed contrarian counter-finding. It is the template for most of what gets published.

## The story they tell

The story Kitces tells about himself is *industry knowledge is fragmented, hard to access, often shallow — and what advisors need to actually improve their practice is research-grade, specific, applicable analysis*. The home page hero — *"Financial planning knowledge to help you better serve your clients. Practice management ideas you can implement yourself."* — frames the offering on two axes: client-facing planning expertise (the technical-knowledge channel) and practice-management know-how (the business-of-being-an-advisor channel). Most industry publications cover one. Kitces covers both, and the audience reads him because the depth on each is unusual.

The implicit competitor is the legacy trade press (FinancialPlanning, ThinkAdvisor, InvestmentNews, FA Mag) and the vendor-sponsored content on those properties. Kitces's positioning relative to that competitive set is *independent, original research with named bylines and an industry-scientist tone* — i.e. not a vendor newsletter dressed up as journalism. The repeated *"We do NOT sell/share any data with advertisers & permit NO third-party marketing. Your email address will be used solely for Kitces communication."* line in the newsletter sign-up modal is a direct shot at the trade-press business model.

The worldview being invited: *the financial planning profession is on an upward trajectory toward something more like a real fiduciary profession (medicine, law) and away from sales/commission roots, and the path to that future is more rigorous research, better practice management, deeper technical knowledge, and a community of practitioners who take the craft seriously enough to call themselves something different ("advicers" rather than "advisors"). If you read here long enough, you will be a more competent professional.* That is a real worldview and it has shaped a generation of fee-only RIAs.

For our entry, the relevant takeaway is that this worldview is also *the air our intended peer firms breathe*. When we go to market and write our positioning, we are writing to and for an industry that has internalized large parts of the Kitces frame — fee transparency norms, the niche-firm thesis, the "comprehensive financial planning" definition, the advisor-as-fiduciary standard.

## What they promise

The hierarchy of promises a visitor sees:

1. **Primary** (the headline above the fold): *"Financial planning knowledge to help you better serve your clients. Practice management ideas you can implement yourself."* — *we will make you better at both halves of your job.*
2. **Secondary** (the audience-count claim, repeated 4x on the page): *"78,507 financial advicers stay up to date with the latest from the Nerd's Eye View. JOIN YOUR FELLOW ADVICERS AND SEE WHAT WE'RE TALKING ABOUT."* — *you should be here because everyone you respect already is.*
3. **Tertiary** (the lead magnet): *"Join over 78,507 fellow advisors now…& receive a free copy of our report: Quantifying the Value of Financial Planning Advice."* — *we will give you a piece of premium research for free if you give us your email.*

Note what is *not* promised on the home page: no AUM claims, no return claims, no "trusted by," no client outcome stories. The promise is consistently educational, not financial.

## How they ask for the sale

The call-to-action strategy:

- **Primary CTA (free funnel)**: *"GET BLOG UPDATES!"* — olive-green button on the email-capture form, repeated at top of page, in dismissable overlay, and in footer modal. The funnel-entry action is "give us your email."
- **Secondary CTA (paid funnel)**: *"BECOME MEMBER"* in the top nav, with two paths — Individual or Group subscription.
- **Tertiary CTAs (event/course)**: *"REGISTER NOW"* (Marketing Summit), *"LEARN MORE"* (new training course), *"TAKE THE SURVEY"* (2026 research survey).
- **Sales-page-equivalents**: each of the four Kitces Report verticals (AdvisorTech, FP Productivity, Marketing, Wellbeing) has a dedicated landing page reached from the RESEARCH dropdown. The CE Catalog functions as a product catalog page.
- **Friction level**: very low at the top of the funnel (one-field email signup), much higher at the paid tier (subscription pricing is not visible on the homepage — must navigate into the member funnel).
- **What this tells us about their sales motion**: a textbook content-marketing funnel. Lead magnet → free newsletter → paid subscription / paid course / paid event. The free product (the blog and podcast) is so substantial and frequent that it works as both customer acquisition and customer retention — paid subscribers stay paid because the free stream keeps reminding them why Kitces is worth subscribing to.

## Who vouches for them

How they prove they're trustworthy:

- **"Nerd's Eye View Praise"** section, repeated twice on the homepage, quoting six third-party "top blog" rankings: *"Top 10 Influential Blog for Financial Advisors,"* *"5 Must-Read Blogs for Financial Advisors,"* *"7 Financial Advisory Blogs that rock,"* *"#1 of Top 10 Industry Blogs,"* *"10 Blogs and Bloggers for Financial Advisors to Read,"* *"Best of the Best for Advisors."*
- **Above-fold partner/award logo strip**: Plan Sponsor, Investopedia, ThinkAdvisor, RIABiz, smartasset, Financial Social Media — each with a brief "Top Influential Blog" / "Top Blog" caption.
- **Audience scale claim**: *78,507 financial advicers* (repeated four times — the most-repeated single number on the site).
- **Named-byline authors**: each post has a named author, often with a date going back years. The "Popular Posts" widget shows posts from 2013, 2015, 2018, 2021, 2022, 2025 — long-running content reinforces archive depth as a trust signal.
- **Implicit personal authority**: Michael himself. The Kitces name is the brand; if you know the industry you know him, and if you don't know him the site assumes you'll figure it out by reading.
- **Speaking circuit visibility**: *"Out And About"* sidebar widget shows upcoming speaking engagements (May 12 Wealthbox webinar, May 21 LaSalle St. webinar, May 26 FPA NorCal in San Francisco) — proof he is being booked by industry partners constantly.
- **Editorial-independence promise**: *"We do NOT sell/share any data with advertisers & permit NO third-party marketing. Your email address will be used solely for Kitces communication."*
- **What's conspicuously *missing***: no client testimonials (correct — there are no retail clients), no "trusted by X firms" logos at the firm level, no AUM-related credibility numbers, no individual subscriber testimonials. The trust signals are entirely *publication-class* (other media calling them the best) rather than *firm-class* (clients saying they did well by us). Right answer for a publication, but worth flagging that the playbook does not transfer to a wealth firm.

## How they price

How pricing is presented:

- **Free tier (Nerd's Eye View blog + podcast + newsletter)**: clearly free, no email wall on individual posts.
- **The Kitces Report (subscription)**: pricing is **gated** — not on the homepage, not in the nav. Must enter the member funnel to see tier and price. The visible group-subscription paywall message — *"Please contact your Firm's Group Admin"* — confirms a group/firm tier exists.
- **CE courses**: also gated. Catalog visible, individual course pricing is on the course page.
- **Live events**: Marketing Summit, IAR Ethics CE Day, etc. — registration page must be opened to see pricing.
- **Speaking and consulting (Michael personally)**: standard pro-services gating; price by quote.

Pricing transparency is **low** by 2026 SaaS-publication standards. Substack would put a $/month next to every paid newsletter; Kitces's site reads more like a traditional industry research subscription (think Cerulli, Gartner) where pricing is a member-only conversation. For a B2B research audience this is normal; for a consumer-facing brand it would be a red flag. Not directly relevant to our pricing decisions, because we are not selling research.

## What they believe

The worldview underneath everything:

1. **Financial planning is a real, technical profession** — closer to medicine and law than to retail sales. The path forward for the industry is rigor, fiduciary standards, named expertise, and continuous education. The deliberate spelling "advicer" is a rhetorical commitment to this view: it forks the label away from the salesperson-coded "advisor" and toward something closer to "practitioner."
2. **Knowledge asymmetries inside the industry are the real bottleneck on practice quality.** Most advisors are not bad people — they are under-resourced, isolated, and not given access to good research on how to do their actual jobs well. Closing those asymmetries (through research, CE, community) is the highest-leverage thing the industry can do.
3. **Education-led marketing is the dominant prospect-acquisition channel for fee-only RIAs** — and it works *if you build the funnel correctly.* This belief is so durably held it shows up in essentially every post about marketing on the site, and is the entire premise of the Marketing Summit.
4. **Editorial independence is non-negotiable.** The repeated "no third-party marketing, no data sales" copy and the absence of advertorial content say *the only way this works is if you trust we are not selling you to vendors.*
5. **The industry can and should self-organize beyond the trade-association legacy.** Beyond Kitces.com itself, Michael co-founded the XY Planning Network (a B2B network for fee-only RIAs serving Gen X/Y clients, structured to make it economically viable for new advisors to serve younger, less-asset-rich clients on a monthly-retainer/subscription basis). XYPN is not part of Kitces.com proper but is part of how Michael's worldview shows up structurally — and it directly supports new RIAs like our user entering the market. *(industry context, not in captured source data)*

## What this means for our entry

### What's working for them

- **A single distinctive vocabulary word ("advicer") doing positioning work everywhere.** One re-spelled word is the entire fork from the mainstream category. We should look for our own equivalent — a single word or phrase that makes the prospect feel they have found a different kind of firm.
- **The lead magnet is a *report* with a category-defining title** ("Quantifying the Value of Financial Planning Advice"). It is not a generic "5 Tips" PDF. It earns the email because it claims to settle a question the prospect actually wonders about. Our lead magnet, if we have one, should be similarly grounded in a real research-shaped question for the segment.
- **Subscriber-count as the dominant trust signal**, repeated four times on the home page. For a publication this is the right number to brag about. For us, the equivalent is "households served," "clients in segment X," or — given we are pre-launch — a deliberate non-number that gets replaced when we have one.
- **Named bylines with personality.** Every post carries a specific named author. For us, naming the actual people on the team (not just "our team") is the cheap, high-leverage version.
- **Multi-channel content with one identity at the center.** Blog + podcast(s) + newsletter + courses + live events + research reports + a public speaking circuit — but always Michael's voice, always one brand. We will probably not match this in year one, but the pattern (one human voice across multiple surfaces) is right.
- **Editorial-independence positioning as a trust differentiator.** "We do NOT sell/share any data with advertisers." If we ever build content, this is a free moat against the vendor-sponsored content the rest of the industry produces.

### Where they're vulnerable

The framing matters here. Kitces is not vulnerable as a business that competes with us. The "vulnerabilities" are gaps in *the category narrative he shapes* that an entrant like us could meaningfully fill in our own corner of the market:

- **The site is unmistakably designed for industry insiders.** The "FOR CONSUMERS" link is two cosmetic items pointing at one referral page. There is essentially no good consumer-facing financial planning content under the Kitces brand — even though Michael is one of the most knowledgeable voices in the field. Any consumer-focused independent RIA that produces *Kitces-quality writing about planning for the actual prospect* is operating in white space at the prospect level. (XY Planning Network firms have started filling this, but it remains thin.)
- **The visual identity is dated by 2026 standards.** Dense single-column blog feed, three blues plus an olive-green CTA, ubiquitous Lato at 400, tight buttons. This is durable for an established publication but unmissable as "older internet" if benchmarked against modern wealth/fintech brands. A new RIA can credibly position as the *modern* face of the Kitces frame.
- **Vocabulary is sealed inside the profession.** "Advicer," "NUA," "Monte Carlo retirement projection," "AdvisorTech" — these are all words a retail prospect either doesn't know or finds alienating. We can take Kitces's *substance* (rigorous, research-led) and translate the *vocabulary* (plain-English, lay-reader-respectful).

### How well do they serve each of our target segments?

Reminder: this section is asking about *prospect-level* service, which is not what Kitces is built for. Scoring accordingly:

- **Early adults setting up proper investment accounts**: **Not aimed at this segment.** No prospect-facing content, no acquisition surface, no onboarding. The XY Planning Network (Michael's adjacent venture) is the actual channel for this segment — and our market-entry should treat XYPN as a separate ecosystem player worth its own profile.
- **40–50 year olds focused on 401(k) management**: **Not aimed at this segment.** Same answer. Mid-career 401(k) prospects who happen to find a Kitces blog post via Google will encounter dense advisor-facing prose and be redirected to "find an advicer." Not a competitive funnel for client acquisition.

The honest read for both segments: Kitces is invisible to retail prospects, and the threat to our acquisition motion is essentially zero. The threat to our *positioning* is that the advisors we'll be benchmarked against by industry observers, podcasters, and peer-press are all reading Kitces, so the frames we use will be evaluated against the frames he's already published.

### What we should consider taking from them

- **The email-first capture funnel.** Not the visual design — the structural decision that the first conversion is *give us your email for a useful free thing*, not *book a call.* For a non-HNW segment especially (early adults), this is the lower-friction first step.
- **Named-byline writing.** When we publish, attach a real human's name and photo. Anonymous "our team" copy is the lowest-trust signal in the category.
- **A category-defining lead magnet.** "Quantifying the Value of Financial Planning Advice" is a category-shaping title. If we publish a research-shaped piece — say, "What Modern Tooling Actually Saves a 401(k)-Heavy Mid-Career Saver" — we can capture a peer-press citation in addition to a prospect's email.
- **One distinctive vocabulary word.** Kitces's "advicer" is a one-word positioning statement. We should look for ours.
- **Editorial-independence promise.** "We do NOT sell/share any data with advertisers" copy translates directly to "We do NOT receive product commissions; our fees come from clients only" for a fee-only RIA.

### What we should deliberately *not* do

- **Write for the industry.** Our writing must be unmistakably aimed at the prospect. If a Kitces reader would find our copy comfortable, we have the wrong reader.
- **Use industry jargon ("advicer," "NUA," "Monte Carlo," "RSU," "tax-loss harvesting") without translation.** Kitces gets to use this language because his readers already know it. We are talking to people who do not.
- **Adopt a 2010s blog-feed homepage.** Kitces's layout is durable because the audience has tolerated it for fifteen years. We do not have that earned tolerance and the format reads as old on first impression.
- **Lean on "X subscribers" as the lead trust signal.** Pre-launch we don't have a number, and even post-launch our trust signals will be client-class (households served, results delivered) not publication-class (newsletter signups).
- **Argue with him publicly.** If we have a positioning that touches anything he has published on (most things), the right move is to read his version first, agree where we genuinely agree, and only diverge with a clear reason. Public disagreement with Kitces is fought on his home field and is rarely net-positive for a small new entrant.
