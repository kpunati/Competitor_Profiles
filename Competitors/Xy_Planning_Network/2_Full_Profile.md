# XY Planning Network — Full Profile

*This is the deep dive. For a quick read, see `1_Summary.md`.*

## The big picture

XY Planning Network (XYPN) is not a wealth-management firm. It is a B2B platform whose customer is the independent fee-only RIA — the very kind of firm we are about to become. Founded in 2014 by Alan Moore and Michael Kitces, XYPN took the unromantic operational scaffolding around a small RIA — compliance filings, technology procurement, marketing playbooks, peer community, registration support — bundled it into a monthly subscription, and built a movement around it. The home page says it plainly: "the only advisor support system that connects you with everything you need to succeed profitably—including a support network of collaborative peers—and guarantees 100% ownership of your business, client relationships, data, and brand." The headline above the fold — *"Your Firm, Your Terms"* — names the customer (a financial planner) and the promise (autonomy) in five words.

The "XY" in the name was originally Gen X and Gen Y (millennial), the underserved demographic XYPN's earliest member firms set out to court. That niche framing is now muted in the network's own copy — today they market to *fee-only advisors at any phase* rather than to advisors-serving-Gen-X-and-Y specifically — but it left a permanent mark on the membership: XYPN's 2,100+ member firms are disproportionately solo and small-team operators who target younger clients, charge monthly retainers and flat planning fees rather than AUM percentages, and serve the segments traditional wirehouses won't profitably touch. The network is also the political backbone for that style of practice: co-founder Michael Kitces personally leads its lobbying for the fiduciary standard, and the homepage's "Advocacy" section makes the policy posture explicit.

For us, XYPN is two things at once and they have to be analyzed separately. (1) As a *network*, it is the largest single concentration of firms competing for our exact target demographics — collectively, the 2,100+ member RIAs are the most credible direct-retail competitive cohort in our category. (2) As a *vendor*, it is a piece of infrastructure we could plug ourselves into — pay $544/month, accept a tech stack, get a compliance team and a consumer-facing advisor directory, and skip a year of setup work. The strategic question of this profile is not "do we beat them?" but "do we join them?"

## Who they sell to

XYPN sells to two distinct customers, and both matter to us for different reasons.

**Primary customer (B2B): fee-only independent financial advisors building or running their own RIA.** The customer segmentation is made explicit on the home page under "Two Paths to Independence":

- **Emerald path** — *"For advisors who want their RIA their way, with hands-on control of daily operations, plus the flexibility to add or remove Support Services and Tool Kits as your needs evolve."* This is the advisor who registers and owns their own RIA, and pays XYPN for the support stack around it.
- **Sapphire path** — *"For advisors who prefer the structure of tucking under a corporate RIA—paired with built-in support that lifts back-office burdens."* This is the advisor who doesn't want to be their own CCO and registers under XYPN's RIA instead.

The audience is explicitly entrepreneurial: phrases on the home page include *"Launch, run and grow the fee-only firm of your dreams,"* *"Your Firm, Your Terms,"* *"100% OWNERSHIP OF YOUR BUSINESS, DATA & CLIENT RELATIONSHIPS,"* and (from a testimonial) *"I would not have been able to enter the industry as a career changer without educational resources like XYPN Academy and compliance support."* The implied buyer profile: an advisor who is (a) leaving a wirehouse / broker-dealer to go independent, or (b) entering financial planning from another career, or (c) running a small existing fee-only RIA and trying to scale. Median member firm appears to be solo or near-solo — implied by the per-advisor pricing structure and the "Additional advisors at the same firm can sign up for an Add-On Membership for an exclusive discount" note on the pricing page.

**Secondary customer (B2C): retail prospects looking for a financial advisor.** This is the *Find an Advisor* directory — a CFP®-designated search portal at `connect.xyplanningnetwork.com/find-an-advisor`, linked from every page. The pitch to advisors describes it as *"Set it and Forget it Lead Generation"*: *"Prospects can find and connect with you easily through our CFP®-designated Find An Advisor search portal."* The pitch to retail prospects is light-touch — the contact page lists *"I'm looking for a financial advisor"* as one of six self-routing buckets and points them to the directory. There is no consumer brand campaign for the directory that we captured; it appears to be primarily organic / direct-search traffic to a structured database of member firms.

**How well does this serve our target segments?** This is where XYPN matters most. The XYPN member network is by design overweight on advisors who target Gen X and Gen Y / millennials — both of our named target segments. A retail prospect who Googles "fee-only fiduciary financial advisor for someone in their 30s" lands disproportionately in the XYPN directory. The directory is, in effect, the dominant aggregated competitor for our two target demographics — not XYPN itself, but the 2,100+ small firms it routes prospects to.

## How they look

In the first three seconds, XYPN reads modern, optimistic, mid-tech-startup, *not* traditional wealth management. The hero on the home page is a high-resolution photograph of a young woman with glasses laughing while holding a coffee mug — explicitly aspirational lifestyle imagery, not boardroom imagery. The dominant background is white with deep saturated cobalt blue accents and a notable lime-green secondary. Buttons are pill-shaped (border-radius 999px). The mood: a B2B SaaS brand that happens to serve advisors.

- **Colors**: white (`rgb(255, 255, 255)`) and warm off-white (`rgb(250, 249, 248)`) carry most of the page. The primary brand color is a strong cobalt blue (`rgb(40, 80, 190)` ≈ `#2850BE`) used for buttons and link accents; a deep navy (`rgb(22, 30, 116)` ≈ `#161E74`) provides contrast for headers and the very-top promo strip; an unusually bright lime-green (`rgb(183, 221, 121)` ≈ `#B7DD79`) appears as a section break and is also the second loop of the wordmark. The cobalt-and-lime combination is the most distinctive design choice — it deliberately signals "newer school" against the navy-and-gold convention of traditional advisory firms.
- **Type**: headings in `Work Sans` at weight **200** (extra-light), body in `Open Sans` at 400. Body size 16.2px; paragraphs 14px. The extra-light heading weight is the second-most distinctive choice — it borrows from contemporary editorial/SaaS design and reads as far less corporate than the bold serifs or heavy sans-serifs that dominate the wealth category. Pairs intentionally with the cobalt accent to feel modern and confident without feeling aggressive.
- **Imagery**: real-people photography, candid and warm. Female advisor in the hero (with named member quotes throughout: Jenn Steliga, Allen Mueller, Cynthia Meyer, Natalie Slagle, Brooklyn Brock, Kaysian Gordon, Cathy Curtis, Bradley Hilton, Terri Spath, Jessica McDonald, Steven Morneau, Tim Gardner, Ben Wacek). Diverse skew toward younger advisors and women — a deliberate counter to the silver-haired-couple imagery of the rest of the category. Photography is the strongest signal of *who they think their member is*.
- **Layout**: spacious, generous section padding, two- and three-column grids for benefit modules. Card-based UI for testimonials (member photo, name, firm name, "XYPN MEMBER SINCE 20XX" — that timestamp is a clever loyalty signal). Document width caps at ~1440px on desktop. Mobile site loads cleanly and reorganizes the cards vertically.
- **Logo**: lowercase "xypn" wordmark with the X cross stroke replaced by a small cobalt circle and an offset lime-green loop — playful, intentionally lower-case, reads more *startup* than *advisor network*. Tagline beneath: "XY PLANNING NETWORK."
- **Primary button**: solid cobalt blue (`rgb(40, 80, 190)`) background, white text, fully pilled (`border-radius: 999px`), small padding (`9.72px 14.4px`). The full pill shape is a 2020s design convention — once again, distinctly *not* the sharp-corner button of the establishment wealth brands.

## The story they tell

XYPN positions itself as the *infrastructure of the fee-only independence movement*. The story has three layers stacked on top of each other.

Layer one is *operational*: the financial-planning profession has shifted from commissioned product-sale to fee-only fiduciary advice, but the operational scaffolding to run a fee-only RIA at a small scale is still bespoke, expensive, and lonely. XYPN exists to centralize that scaffolding. "We've put their expertise and first-hand knowledge to work for you by vetting and negotiating enterprise-priced partnerships with the top technology for RIA owners," reads the Tech Tools section.

Layer two is *cultural and political*: the network claims a mission to *advance the profession* — Kitces leads advocacy at the state and SEC levels for the fiduciary standard. The home page is explicit: *"XYPN fights for the fiduciary standard of excellence and the establishment of regulation in the consumer's best interest… we are actively working to advance public policy that recognizes financial planning as a distinct and respected profession—separate from the essential but different roles of financial product manufacturers and distributors."* This is positioning XYPN against wirehouses and product-selling broker-dealers, not against other RIAs.

Layer three is *emotional*: independence is hard, isolating, and overwhelming, and XYPN's job is to make you feel less alone. *"You may be independent, but you're never alone when you plug into the Network."* The testimonial bank is full of variations on this: *"The community aspects of XYPN help firm owners feel like we're not on an island,"* and *"XYPN made getting my time back possible. I'm able to be more present as a parent."* Membership is sold partly on logistics and partly on belonging.

The implicit competitor they're swinging at is the **wirehouse / broker-dealer career path** — the world an advisor leaves to come build their own fee-only firm. XYPN is "what you do *instead* of staying at Merrill." A secondary implicit competitor is the **PE-backed RIA aggregators** (Focus Partners, Mariner, Creative Planning) that buy advisors out of independence: *"your practice and its value always belong to you. If you're not satisfied, you can exit at any time with your clients and 100% of your revenue."* That sentence is engineered to contrast with the multi-year non-competes and equity-roll structures of the aggregator deals.

The hierarchy of promises a visitor sees on the home page:

1. **Primary** (the H1 above the fold): *"Your Firm, Your Terms."* Subhead: *"Launch, run and grow the fee-only firm of your dreams with complete autonomy."* The promise is ownership and self-determination.
2. **Secondary** (the three pillar stats below the hero): **100%** ownership of business/data/clients · **2,100+** like-minded advisors · **90%** high-satisfaction rate. Each one validates a different promise — control, community, quality.

The repeated capstone — *"XYPN Makes it Possible"* — is positioned as the brand's permission statement. Every dream you have about your firm is technically possible; XYPN is the bridge that makes it *practically* possible.

## How they price

- **Transparent**. This is one of the most striking facts in our captured set: the pricing page leads with a literal **$544/month** above-the-fold number, and the compliance page lists eight separate price points with no obfuscation. That alone differentiates XYPN against most of the wealth-management category, where pricing is gated behind a discovery call.
- **Tier names**: Emerald (own-RIA, $544/mo) and Sapphire (tuck-in, undisclosed). Compliance tiers named Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3 — Dedicated Compliance Advisor / Compliance Program Manager / Integrated Compliance Partner. These names ladder up clearly.

The pitch reframes the $544 from a cost into a discount — buy XYPN and net positive on tech savings alone. This is a deliberate framing move that lets them publish a real price without making it feel expensive.

## What they believe

Three convictions ladder up to the brand:

1. **Fee-only fiduciary advice is the correct future of financial planning, and the wirehouse/broker-dealer model is the wrong past.** The whole site is staked on this; the advocacy section makes it institutional.
2. **Small, independent, advisor-owned RIAs serve clients better than scale RIAs.** Hence the 100%-ownership pledge, the no-non-compete posture, and the explicit anti-PE framing.
3. **Operational scaffolding should be commoditized so the advisor can focus on the relationship.** Tech, compliance, lead-gen, and education are infrastructure — not differentiators. The differentiator is the advisor's relationship with the client.

The underlying worldview: the financial-planning profession is too small, too lonely, and too fragmented for any single small firm to economically build everything it needs. Pooling resources (tech procurement, compliance expertise, lobbying, community) at network scale makes solo independence actually viable. The network's mission is to remove every reason an advisor might say "I can't go independent."

## What this means for our entry

This is the most complicated entry analysis in our set because XYPN is simultaneously a competitor, a vendor, and a category-shaper. We have to think about all three.

### What's working for them

- **Published pricing is a moat in a category that hides it.** $544/month is in plain text, above the fold. Every prospect can self-qualify before they ever talk to sales. We should assume their pricing-disclosure stance is a meaningful lead-quality and conversion-rate advantage.
- **Two-customer architecture done cleanly.** Advisors and retail prospects use the same site but follow different paths — the contact page literally has six self-routing buckets ("I'm interested in Emerald" / "...Sapphire" / "I'm looking for a financial advisor" / "I'm looking for an industry expert" / "...partnerships" / "...press"). This is a model for how to segment a multi-audience landing experience without losing focus.
- **The "Find an Advisor" directory as a member benefit.** Lead-generation reframed as part of the product. Member advisors don't have to do their own SEO from scratch; the network does it once at scale. This is genuinely sticky.
- **Real, named, headshot-attributed member testimonials with firm names and join-dates.** Every quote is verifiable. The "XYPN MEMBER SINCE 20XX" line on each testimonial is a small but effective tenure-trust signal.
- **Modern visual identity that signals "not your father's advisor."** Cobalt + lime, extra-light Work Sans, pilled buttons, candid photography, lowercase wordmark. The whole brand is a deliberate counter-signal to silver-haired-couple wealth design. For us, this is the validated playbook — modern visual identity *can* work in financial planning without sacrificing credibility, *if* you back it with named experts and explicit fiduciary positioning.
- **Owning a phrase ("Make it possible")** that closes every page. Cheap, repeatable, defensible.

### Where they're vulnerable

- **The brand's "fee-only / Gen X / Gen Y" original niche has been diluted.** The "XY" in the name no longer maps to the demographic in any meaningful way on the site. The homepage hero talks about *advisors,* not about the *kinds of clients those advisors serve.* That leaves the demographic positioning open for the actual member firms (and competitors of those firms) to capture downstream.
- **The retail surface (Find-an-Advisor directory) is weak.** It's framed to the advisor as "set it and forget it lead generation" — which is itself an admission that XYPN is not putting heavy marketing effort behind making consumers aware of it. A retail prospect Googling "millennial financial advisor" is more likely to land on an individual member firm's site than on the XYPN directory. There's no XYPN consumer brand campaign, no XYPN-branded content for retail readers, no XYPN editorial property aimed at end clients.
- **The Sapphire tuck-in offering creates an internal tension.** XYPN's whole story is "100% ownership of your business, data, clients, and brand," but the Sapphire model — where the advisor registers under XYPN's RIA — is by definition *less than* 100% structural ownership. The Sapphire pricing is conspicuously absent from the pricing page, suggesting the network knows this is a more delicate sale.
- **No collective AUM number.** They lead with "2,100+ firms" but never publish "$X billion managed across the network." That's likely because the average member firm is small enough that the network total doesn't read impressively against the aggregator competitors. Vulnerable to a "but how much do they actually manage?" question.
- **Members compete with each other on the directory.** A network of 2,100 firms targeting the same demographics is also a network of 2,100 firms competing for the same prospects in the same search portal. XYPN's incentive is to add more members; the median member's incentive is for fewer members in their geography/specialty. This tension shows up as muted in the marketing but is real.
- **Heavy reliance on Michael Kitces personally.** Co-founder is named on the home page; he's the public face of advocacy. If Kitces's reputation or attention shifts, the brand loses an asset.

### How well do they serve each of our target segments?

For each segment in `_context/our_thesis.md`:

- **Early adults setting up proper investment accounts** — **Weak (as XYPN-the-network)** / **Strong (collectively, via the member firm directory)**. XYPN itself doesn't surface as the destination for a 25-year-old looking for their first advisor. But many XYPN member firms are explicitly built to serve this segment with monthly-retainer models and no AUM minimum — those firms will appear in the consumer's search results. Our competition for this segment is the median XYPN member firm, not XYPN itself.
- **40–50 year olds focused on 401(k) management** — **Weak (as XYPN)** / **Strong (collectively, via the directory)**. Same dynamic. The network's member roster includes hundreds of firms that target mid-career professionals with held-away-asset management and 401(k) advice. They will rank in the same search results we will.

### What we should consider taking from them

- **Publish pricing.** Even rough ranges. The category default is to hide it; XYPN's success suggests the default is wrong, at least for our segments. Specifically consider a single hero number ($X/month or starting-at-$X) above the fold on our primary landing page.
- **The four-quadrant CTA pattern.** XYPN's homepage hero has four buttons: *Start an RIA / Grow your RIA / Join our RIA / Find an advisor* — three lifecycle stages plus a retail entry. We could adapt this for our retail audience: e.g., *Investing in my 20s / Mid-career planning / Pre-retirement / Talk to an advisor.*
- **Named, photographed, dated client testimonials with firm names.** Adapted from XYPN's "MEMBER SINCE 20XX" tenure stamp — a particularly cheap and effective trust signal.
- **A signature short phrase that closes every page.** ("Make it possible" is theirs. We need our own.)
- **Modern visual identity (cobalt + lime, extra-light sans-serif headlines, pilled CTAs).** Less aggressive than fintech, more confident than legacy advisor brands. The middle ground works.
- **The "savings calculator" idea, repurposed for retail.** XYPN's calculator shows advisors how much they'll save in tech costs. A retail equivalent — fee-comparison calculator vs. wirehouse / vs. AUM-percentage advisors — would do similar work for our published-fee positioning.
- **Tiered service architecture** if our entry includes a digital-first early-adult product. XYPN's two-path structure (own-it / tuck-under-ours) is a clean way to ladder a low-friction entry tier to a higher-touch core tier.

### What we should deliberately *not* do

- **Don't pitch to advisors.** We are a retail RIA, not a B2B network. XYPN's voice and positioning is calibrated for advisor buyers — we should resist the gravitational pull to sound like a peer-to-peer industry voice, because our customer is not an advisor.
- **Don't lean on the same "100% ownership / autonomy" frame.** It's structurally a B2B value prop. Translated to retail it becomes vague.
- **Don't try to compete with XYPN on infrastructure breadth.** Their tech, compliance, and community stack is the result of a decade of accrual. If we want any of it (Smartria, vetted vendor partnerships, NAPFA discount), we should *consume* it as a member, not rebuild it.
- **Don't underestimate the directory as a competitive surface.** Even though it's a weak consumer brand on its own, it's an aggregator that puts hundreds of competitors in front of our target prospects. Our SEO and content posture has to assume the prospect has already seen four XYPN-member-firm sites before they reach ours.

### The strategic question: join, compete, or both?

The unique-to-XYPN question this profile must answer. Three options:

1. **Join (become an Emerald member).** Pay $544/month, plug into the tech stack, take the compliance support, get listed in the directory. Costs ~$6,500/year baseline; saves substantially more than that on the tech stack alone if their $7,824 average savings number is honest. Faster time-to-launch, fewer operational distractions. Tradeoff: we look like a member of a crowded network rather than a distinctive new brand. Our visual identity has to stand apart even though our backstage infrastructure is shared.
2. **Compete (build our own infrastructure and stay outside).** Higher upfront cost in tech procurement and compliance setup. Brand-wise more distinctive; we don't appear as one of 2,100 dots on a network map. Tradeoff: more setup overhead, no peer community baked in, no inherited lead-gen from the directory.
3. **Both (use XYPN as infrastructure but compete with member firms on the retail surface).** Practically: join for the operational scaffolding, but don't rely on the Find-an-Advisor directory as a customer-acquisition channel. Build our own retail brand, content engine, and SEO presence independently. This is probably the dominant strategy for a pre-launch firm with a serious tech-and-data thesis.

**Recommended read**: option 3, with a planned-exit timeline. Join in year one to compress setup time, keep our consumer brand fully independent and don't relegate our marketing to the directory, and re-evaluate membership at year 2–3 once we've built enough internal capability to consider standing alone. The risk we're managing for is becoming structurally dependent on XYPN's stack such that we can't credibly differentiate on our claimed edge — *modern data and tech tooling* — when their tech stack is the same one every other XYPN member uses.
