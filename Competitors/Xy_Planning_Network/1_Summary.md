# XY Planning Network — Summary

> A B2B subscription network — not an RIA — that sells operational infrastructure (compliance, tech, community, lead-gen directory) to 2,100+ small fee-only independent advisor firms for $544/month, many of which target our exact Gen X/Y demographics.

## The headline

XYPN doesn't compete with us directly — they sell the picks and shovels to the firms that will. Co-founded in 2014 by Michael Kitces and Alan Moore, the network is *the* operational backbone of the small fee-only RIA movement and the largest single funnel of advisors competing for our two target demographics. The practical question they pose isn't "how do we beat them?" — it's "do we pay them $544/month for the infrastructure and use it to launch faster?"

## Who they are

- **Founded**: 2014 (XYPN-the-network; the affiliated RIA *Fiduciary Financial Planning Network*, CRD 313361, is more recent — exact date needs ADV Part 1A)
- **Size (network reach)**: 2,100+ member RIA firms (the meaningful commercial number)
- **Size (tuck-in RIA, CRD 313361)**: 5 employees, 5 advisors, **$175.7M AUM** as of 2026-03-31, 2 branches per IAPD. *Not* the network's commercial reach — this is just the operational entity that Sapphire-path members register under.
- **Where they're based**: Onalaska, WI (per IAPD; Bozeman, MT is also historically associated)
- **How they're funded**: Privately held; specifics not in source data
- **Leadership**: Michael Kitces (co-founder, publicly named on home page); Alan Moore (co-founder, industry context)

## What they sell, to whom

To **fee-only independent financial advisors** (their B2B customer): a subscription called **Emerald at $544/month** that bundles compliance support, vetted tech stack, NAPFA membership, community access, education, and a member profile in the Find-an-Advisor directory. A second tier called **Sapphire** lets advisors tuck under XYPN's own RIA instead of registering their own (price not published). Compliance add-ons priced individually: $3,500 to register a new RIA, $575–$2,000/month for ongoing compliance support, $360/hour for à-la-carte consulting.

To **retail prospects** (their secondary B2C surface): the *Find an Advisor* directory at `connect.xyplanningnetwork.com/find-an-advisor`, framed to the advisor side as "Set it and Forget it Lead Generation" — i.e., not a heavily marketed consumer brand, but a structured search portal feeding qualified leads back to the 2,100+ member firms.

The sales motion is content + conference (XYPN LIVE) + ecosystem; pricing is transparent on the site (rare in the category) but enrollment is assisted, not self-serve.

## How big a threat

**Medium-high — with strong nuance.** XYPN itself isn't a retail competitor; they don't pitch retail wealth management. But the 2,100+ member firms they support are collectively the largest source of direct retail competitors for our Gen X/Y demographics in the entire market, and the network's category-shaping work (fiduciary advocacy led by Kitces, the XYPN LIVE conference, published industry research, the fee-only-monthly-retainer business model they've normalized) means they set the rules of the category we're entering. The threat is structural and indirect rather than head-to-head, which is why it doesn't read "high" — but ignoring them would be the bigger error than over-rating them.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Published pricing above the fold ($544/month)** in a category that universally hides it — a deliberate friction-cutter that lets prospects self-qualify before they ever talk to sales.
- **Two-customer architecture done cleanly**: same site, two audiences, six self-routing buckets on the contact page. Advisors and retail prospects are segmented without watering down either path.
- **Real, named, photographed member testimonials with firm names and "MEMBER SINCE 20XX" tenure stamps** — verifiable trust signals, repeated on every page.
- **Modern visual identity that signals "not your father's advisor"** — cobalt + lime palette, extra-light Work Sans headings, pilled buttons, candid young-advisor photography. Validates that modern brand design *can* work in financial planning without sacrificing credibility.
- **Owning a repeated closing phrase ("XYPN Makes it Possible")** that anchors every page — cheap, repeatable, defensible.

## Where they're vulnerable

- The **"XY" demographic positioning has been diluted** — the network no longer leads with "advisors who serve Gen X and Y clients," leaving that consumer-facing positioning open for retail firms (us, and our competitors) to capture downstream.
- The **Find-an-Advisor directory is a weak consumer brand** — XYPN doesn't market the directory to retail prospects, framing it to advisors as "set it and forget it" lead-gen, which is itself an admission of light consumer-side investment.
- The **Sapphire tuck-in model contradicts the "100% ownership" core promise** — and its pricing is conspicuously absent from the public pricing page.
- They **lead with member count (2,100+) but never publish collective AUM** — suggesting the average member firm is small enough that the aggregate doesn't read impressively against PE-backed aggregators.
- **2,100 member firms competing with each other** on the same directory for the same demographics is a structural tension the marketing papers over.

## What this means for our entry

XYPN forces an unusual strategic choice that no other competitor in our set imposes: *do we join the network and use it as infrastructure, or stay outside and build everything ourselves?* The recommended read is **option 3 — join, but with a planned-exit timeline**: pay the $544/month in year one to compress setup time and access vetted tech and compliance, but build our consumer-facing brand and demand-generation pipeline fully independently of the Find-an-Advisor directory, then re-evaluate at year 2–3. The reason this matters more than any single competitor profile: our claimed edge is *modern data and tech tooling*, and if our backstage tech stack is the same XYPN-vetted stack every other member uses, we cannot credibly differentiate on that axis. Joining for the operational scaffolding is fine; relying on XYPN to shape our customer-facing product is not.
