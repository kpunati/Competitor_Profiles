# What is a Flat Fee Financial Advisor?

Financial advisors use many different terms to differentiate their services to potential clients. However, there are no clear definitions of these terms, creating a lot of confusion for people seeking financial advice. We want to take a closer look at one of the newest terms in our industry with hopes of defining it and creating clearer expectations for consumers.

**A Flat Fee financial advisor charges a flat fee for their services and the fee is not dependent on a client’s managed investments, net worth or complexity. In a true flat fee relationship, advisors are compensated with one fixed fee for services and not by any other means such as product sales or commissions**. The flat fees charged by the advisor vary, but typically range from $1,000 to $10,000 per year or $4,000 to $10,000 for a standalone engagement.

Compare this to other industry terms such as fee based or fee only.

-   A [fee based](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/feebasedinvestment.asp) advisor may be compensated both on a percentage of the client’s assets they manage as well as through product sales.
-   A [fee only](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing/102014/feeonly-financial-advisers-what-you-need-know.asp) advisor is compensated from a percentage of the client’s assets they manage, but not product sales.

Rather than getting too caught up in different terminology, the most important thing to understand is how exactly your advisor gets paid, and do you agree with that compensation structure?

This is where the **Flat Fee** structure really shines. It is the most transparent pricing structure, the easiest for consumers to understand, and the one with the least inherent conflicts of interest.

### **Reducing Conflicts of Interest**

If your advisor’s flat fee is not contingent on any of the advice they provide, you can be confident that the advice you’re receiving is transparent and in your best interest. Let’s take a few examples where the difference between advice given by a Flat Fee advisor might differ from other types of advisors.

Consider a scenario where you are wondering whether or not to use money from your investments to pay down high interest debt such as student loans or credit cards. A Flat Fee advisor will conduct an unbiased analysis of your situation and provide you with their best recommendation. An advisor that receives compensation tied to your investments would see their income decrease if you take money out of your investments to pay down the high interest debt – and as a result, they may wrestle with what answer to give you - a clear example of why it is best to avoid conflicts of interest when receiving important financial advice.

Another example can be seen when you are considering making an alternative investment purchase such as a rental property. A Flat Fee advisor’s income does not change regardless of whether you purchase the rental property. A fee only, fee based, or any other advisor whose income is tied to your investments will see their income decrease if you withdraw money from traditional investments to pay for the rental property. Their recommendation may be influenced by THEIR bottom line, not yours.

Lastly, proper insurance coverage remains an important part of all of our lives. A Flat Fee advisor will balance your true insurance needs with the most cost effective policy for your situation, but an advisor who receives commission from the insurance you buy may not recommend the most suitable policy for your needs.

### **Greater Access to Advice**

The payment structure of Flat Fee financial advisors allows them to work with a broader group of clients who would not normally have access to a traditional financial advisor.

Non-Flat Fee advisors may have minimum investment requirements and restrict their services to only people with a certain amount of money, such as having at least $1,000,000 to invest with them. There’s an extremely underserved market of people without a lot of investments, who are still paying off debt and building their wealth, but also need financial advice. Flat Fee advisors can help serve this group.

Not only do Flat Fee financial advisors have the potential to provide services to a greater number of people that need help, they are also able to provide a greater breadth of advice because their revenues are in no way tied to the advice they provide.

If an advisor is compensated only on insurance sales, they may restrict their advice to only what serves that purpose. You may not receive help with budgeting, picking your benefits through work, reducing your taxable income, or planning for future goals like you would working with a Flat Fee advisor.

### **Do Flat Fee Financial Advisors Have Hidden Fees?**

It’s natural to assume that there are hidden costs or some form of undisclosed compensation taking place with Flat Fee advisors. Fortunately, with a true Flat Fee advisor, you can be confident there are no hidden fees or commissions the advisor is receiving. However, you may still incur additional costs related to the investments or insurance coverage that you purchase.

For example, if your Flat Fee advisor recommends you purchase an investment such as the Vanguard 500 Index Fund (VOO), you would still pay an expense ratio of 0.03% on this investment ($3 on a $10,000 investment). This fee would be charged (and received) by Vanguard, not the Flat Fee advisor.

Likewise, if the advisor were to recommend term life insurance that came with a monthly premium of $35, you would still need to pay this premium to the life insurance company. The Flat Fee advisor would not receive commission or any portion of your monthly premium.

### **What are the Downsides of a Flat Fee Financial Advisor?**

There are a lot of positives about the Flat Fee advisor model, but that doesn’t mean it is a perfect fit for everyone. Depending on the advisor, the biggest drawback for a potential client is the Flat Fee might end up being more than you would pay another advisor who has a different compensation model.

For example, if you paid your Flat Fee advisor $6,000 for advice and had $200,000 of investments or [assets under management](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/aum.asp), you would have paid less with an advisor who charged 1% on your investments: $2,000 vs. $6,000. Of course, this amount would go up as your investments grow, while the Flat Fee will remain the same (unless changed by the advisor).

Another drawback is for the advisors who operate within a Flat Fee model. It is significantly less profitable for them compared to traditional Fee Only or Fee Based models. While an advisor’s Flat Fee is capped, an advisor who receives their income off of clients’ investments or product sales theoretically has unlimited income potential.

### **Are there Differences Among Flat Fee Financial Advisors?**

It’s important to understand that all Flat Fee Advisors are not the same. Just as the most important thing to understand about your potential advisor is how they’re paid, the other big thing to know is what advice you will receive for the Flat Fee. Will they advise you in all areas of your financial life, or focus on something in particular such as budgeting or taxes?

Until now, Flat Fee advisors have been broken down into two main categories:

-   Advisors who directly manage your investments
-   Advisors who do not directly manage your investments

This second group is commonly referred to as [**Advice-Only**](https://www.adviceonlynetwork.com/) financial advisors.
