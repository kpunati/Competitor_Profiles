# 5 Questions to Ask Your Real Estate Agent

Your home is usually one of your largest assets. When you are selling a home and using an agent to work on your behalf, we strongly recommend treating it like you’re hiring a key employee for your financial team.

You will want to interview at least 2-3 agents to get a sense for their different perspectives, experience, and working styles. While your friend, cousin, or sibling-in-law may have a real estate license, be careful about assuming you’ll hire them unless they happen to be a top broker in your area with significant experience.

Once you have your potential agents picked out, either from internet research, word of mouth, or your personal network, here are the top 5 questions you can ask to make sure you have found a good fit. 

### **5 Questions To Ask Your Real Estate Agent**

**What is your fee structure and what is included in that fee?**

Real estate commission can be **hefty**. Did you know that if you buy a $500,000 home and later sell it for $550,000, you would on average **break even** strictly on closing costs and agent commissions alone? That is ignoring all the other costs of home ownership! The typical closing costs are 3% and typical agent fees are 6% (split between buying and selling agent)..

However, not all agents operate with the same fees or same fee structure. Just like Abundo operates on a flat-fee model with no percentages, there are actually real estate companies who do the same! If you can find one in your area, they may charge $5,000 or similar type of fixed fee to sell your home. If you have a $500,000 home, that’s an immediate savings of $10,000 off of the industry standard 3% selling agent commission. As an example, we know an agent in Minnesota (Joey Torkildson - friend of Abundo) who works on a flat-fee basis that saves clients a lot of money.

If you work with an agent who operates on a percentage basis, as most still do, remember you still have the ability to negotiate fees. You don’t have to offer the “normal” commission to the agents. Just be aware if you negotiate for lower percentage fees, some agents may not want to work with you or may not show your house with as much enthusiasm. That’s just the reality of the business.

**What will you do to market my home effectively? Can you show me examples of your marketing for a recent home?**

Since you’re paying a healthy amount of money to an agent to broker your deal, you want to feel assured that they are doing enough to earn as much of that commission as possible.

What you want to avoid is an agent who operates more like a tour guide than a salesperson. Opening up the front door for a few buyers and hosting an open house really isn’t a justification to earn a 5 figure paycheck.

Again, think about this like you are interviewing and hiring someone for this job at work. You would want to feel confident they:

-   Are well-versed in multiple modern marketing techniques, including a demonstrated expertise of social media and other other platforms
-   Can describe in detail what features of the home they will use to create an engaging story for potential buyers
-   Have a strong network with other agents in the area to be able to find buyers who might not have stumbled across your listing on their own

**What will your pricing strategy be and how will you sell that price to potential buyers?**

Most negotiations involve some type of comparison or “comp” approach. Real estate is no different. The trick is that there’s no set standard for what the right set of comps actually are.

A good buyer’s agent should be looking for comps that are favorable to their buying clients to increase the probability of a sale at a great buying price. Likewise, your selling agent should be able to articulate a case for why your home is actually comparable to higher priced units because of certain key selling features for the target demographic.

Reality sits at the center of all of this though, so you want to avoid two types of agents: ones who don’t have a good vision or story for how to sell your home convincingly and ones who have pie-in-the-sky claims not grounded in reality. Are you sold on the case your potential agent is making? Do they do a great job highlighting the home’s unique features?

**How many other properties are you currently selling and in what neighborhood or price point?**

Imagine you have a $500,000 home and you come across an agent with dozens of open listings for $1M or more. Yes, that might indicate that the agent has a healthy business, but how much will they focus on your sale? A lot of open listings well above your price point could present a risk of low importance for your sale.

On the flip side, if someone tells you they only have a small number of listings or that their listings are very dissimilar from yours, that would be an indicator they might be lacking in either expertise or experience with your particular market.

The Goldilocks answer here should give you confidence that you would be an important client to this agent and that they also have strong experience with selling homes in your neighborhood and price point.

**What should I know about the process of working together? I’d like to know how it will work from beginning to end.**

I like questions like this for two reasons. First, it’s helpful factual information to know. It’s good to get a sense for all the steps in the process ahead of time for planning purposes.

More importantly than that, though, is that this is a great question to listen for the hidden answer. How good is this agent at communicating with you?

If an agent is able to speak in terms that you understand, teach you things about the process, be kind and empathetic, and treat your “basic” question as if it matters, those are great signs that you’ll have someone who is responsive, articulate, and helpful in the future. If they are in any way dismissive, vague, or unhelpful in answering a question like this, that’s a great sign that they aren’t the person for you.
