# Is Tax Loss Harvesting Right for You?

*Tax loss harvesting gets a lot of hype!  It can be useful—but it doesn’t always deliver big bucks, and in fact it’s easy to misstep and end up costing more than you save. Read on for the truth beyond the hype, and see if TLH makes sense for you.*

### **What Is Tax Loss Harvesting?**

We all invest in hopes of *earning* money, so of course it's a disappointment when your stock, fund, or crypto actually *loses* money instead.  The silver lining is that you can get a tax break!

Tax loss harvesting is **intentionally selling investments that have lost money** in order to capture tax savings. Remember that just because your investment has gone down in value (“on paper”), that doesn’t entitle you to claim the tax loss. You have to actually sell it.

### **What are the Benefits of Tax Loss Harvesting?**

**(1) Save on Gains.** You can first cut your taxes by offsetting any taxable *gains,* if you had better luck with other investments. Let’s say you sold one stock at a profit and have a $15,000 taxable capital gain. But you also have another stock in your brokerage account that has *lost* $15,000. You can **harvest this loss** by selling the stock to fully offset your capital gain—erasing your tax bill!  That would be a $2,250 win if you’re in the 15% capital gains bracket.

**(2) Save on Income.** If you don’t have any gains, or if your losses exceed your gains, there’s an even better tax hack available. You can take up to $3,000 as a deduction against your *ordinary income* (which is normally taxed at a higher rate). Imagine you had that same $15,000 loss above, but no taxable gains. If you’re in the 22% income tax bracket, you could take the 3K deduction and save $660 this year—plus the IRS would allow you to carry over the remaining $12,000 of losses to future years.  Over 5 years, you could save $3,300.

### **But Isn’t It a Bad Idea to Sell in a Down Market?**

Abundo’s investing philosophy focuses on “staying the course.” That means when stocks temporarily drop in value, we don’t advocate selling them. Selling low during fearful times costs a lot of investors a lot of money over the long term.

What makes tax loss harvesting different is that you will ideally **sell and then immediately reinvest**. That way you stay in the market, ready for recovery.  Step 1 is to sell your stock, crypto, or mutual fund/ETF to realize a tax loss.  Step 2 is to immediately purchase another investment that is similar —or better—but not exactly the same. So you’re not actually out of the market, except for the very short time (ideally minutes) between your sell and buy trades.

### **Why Is It Important to Buy a Similar Fund Instead of the Same One?**

The IRS is wise to the idea that someone could take advantage of tax loss harvesting by selling a stock, realizing a loss for tax benefits, and then buying the exact same stock one second later. That would allow the person to get a tax break without any investment risk.

Enter the **WASH SALE RULE**. The IRS does not let you claim a loss if you buy an asset that is “substantially identical” to the one sold. Any purchase of a “substantially identical” investment within 30 days **before or after** the loss (so a 61-day window) will disallow the loss.  Now, if you are selling an investment to change your portfolio—for example, selling an individual stock to buy an index fund—there’s no problem.  But what about if you’re already in good funds and want to keep what you own?

**Similar but not identical** funds are the solution.  Note the IRS does not actually define “substantially identical,” so you will have to make a judgment call, but you can aim to buy a fund that shares just *some* overlap with the one you sold.  One approach investors use is to buy a fund that tracks a different branded index.  For example, in the category of total international stock, Vanguard’s **VXUS** follows the *“FTSE Global All Cap ex US Index"* (~8,000 stocks) while iShares’ **IXUS** follows the *“MSCI ACWI ex USA IMI Index”* (~4,000 stocks).  So you could sell VXUS and buy IXUS to harvest a loss but stay invested in international markets.  (A similar pair is available for US stocks: **VTI** & **ITOT**.)

Or, if you want to play it safe and choose something even less similar, Vanguard’s **VEA** targets just international *developed markets* (not emerging), so it has only about 75% overlap with **VXUS**.  Similarly, **VOO** targets just *US large-cap* stocks (the S&P 500), so it has only about 85% overlap with **VTI**.

### **Are There Any Downsides or Pitfalls?**

  
Tax loss harvesting is a complex investing action, and it can fail in several ways.   

###### *Tax loss harvesting is a time sensitive activity!* 

1.  **MISSING THE BOAT** You have to move fast, because losses you see today can vanish in an hour or a day.  If a strong recovery erases the loss, that’s of course not really a *problem*—your investments grew back!  But it is very much a problem if you don’t notice and sell anyway, because then your sale could accidentally trigger a gain instead of a loss. Then you’ve ***added*** **to your tax bill instead of cutting it.**  Note: This is especially risky if trading after-hours or with mutual funds, since the sale won’t execute until later—and you don’t know what the price will be then. 
2.  **ONLY TAXABLE BROKERAGE ACCOUNTS**. If all your money is invested in retirement accounts, like 401(k)s and IRAs, or other tax-advantaged accounts like 529s, you cannot tax loss harvest.   
3.  **AUTOMATIC WASH SALES.** Oops! You can accidentally trigger a wash sale—**erasing your tax savings**—without even realizing it.  Do you have automated contributions into the same stock/fund you’re selling **in any accounts**, inc. RSU grants or retirement accounts like 401(k) and IRA?  Those accounts aren’t available to harvest losses—but they do count against you for wash sales!  Similarly, do you have automated dividend reinvestment in any accounts?  That technically counts as a purchase, too, so you may need to turn these off.
4.  **UNINVESTED CASH**. That said, turning off automatic investments & reinvestments can easily cost you more money than you save from tax loss harvesting!  You now become responsible for manually investing any cash—so are you going to remember to keep up with that? If dividends sit around in cash and you forget to manually reinvest them, they won’t grow with the market.  This problem compounds if you forget to go back later and turn the automatic investments & reinvestments back on!
5.  **SELLING THE WRONG LOTS**. If you’re not selling an entire position (for example, if you own $10,000 of Amazon stock but only want to sell $5,000), it really matters that you choose the correct tax lots!  Each past purchase you made (inc. dividends) counts as a separate “lot” with its own cost basis—and some of those might be at a loss while others are at a gain.  You’ll need to edit the trade request to sell the right lots.  (Or, only attempt tax loss harvesting when your entire position is down.)
6.  **WASTING A LOSS**.  Should you be harvesting *gains* instead of losses?  It’s uncommon, but if your income is low enough to put you in the 0% capital gains bracket, it might help you more to harvest tax-free *gains*.  Alternatively, are there wrinkles in your state & local tax that could interfere with claiming a loss? (For example, [Pennsylvania](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/revenue/documents/formsandpublications/papersonalincometaxguide/documents/pitguide_netgainssaleexchngdispprop.pdf) currently does not allow carryover to future years.)
7.  **TAX LOSS HARVESTING IS TAX DEFERRAL.** It does not eliminate taxes. When you sell and then immediately rebuy similar investments at depressed prices, you are lowering your cost basis. Assuming markets recover and continue upward, when you sell in the future, more of your proceeds will be taxable capital gains due to your new lower investment basis. This tax-deferral can be advantageous or work against you, depending on your specific circumstances.

### **Is Tax Loss Harvesting Worth The Effort?**

  
Like many things in personal finance, it depends!   

###### *The financial industry has a large incentive to oversell the benefits of tax loss harvesting.* 

Investors hate paying taxes and love the idea of getting one over on the system, plus tax loss harvesting is just complex enough that RoboAdvisors or AUM advisors can win clients by promising substantial tax savings with this method.

However, the reality is that the benefits are pretty modest for most people. With the $3,000 per year deduction limit, most people are getting anywhere from about $400-$1,000 per year of tax savings depending on their bracket. And some of those savings are lost if you sell the replacement shares later, because you will now have a larger capital gain embedded in those.

Ben Felix, Chief Investment Officer at PWL Capital and one of the best personal finance podcasters in the business, estimated an **annual benefit of 0.05%-0.23% from perfectly optimized tax loss harvesting**. That is to say a 7% return could be juiced up to 7.05%-7.23% in the ideal scenario. Not exactly life-changing stuff—it’s more like the cherry on top than the ice cream. (You can [**listen to his full podcast here**](https://rationalreminder.ca/podcast/158), which is excellent.)

### **When Is Tax Loss Harvesting Likely to be Worth It?**

  
There are a few boxes to check to get the maximum benefit for the least amount of effort. If all of these apply, you might be an ideal candidate for tax loss harvesting:  

-   You have a **large unrealized loss** in your taxable account
-   You're in a high tax bracket (22% or above)
-   You are willing to have a messier portfolio, including the replacement funds
-   You expect to eventually sell or gift the replacement at a very low tax rate (sell in a 0% capital gains rate retirement year, donate to charity, or leave to heirs)
-   You are willing to go through the effort to avoid wash sales across all your accounts, and to restore automated investments & dividend reinvestments as soon as the 61-day window is up

  
If all of those circumstances apply, you could make one large loss sale, take the $3,000 tax deduction for many years, and then stop worrying about it. That’s really the ideal scenario. Of course, if you love tinkering with your portfolio, you could harvest smaller losses—but that’s not going to be something 95% of people prefer to spend their time doing.  And given the ease of making a mistake…   

###### *You might be picking up pennies in front of a steamroller!*

### **Final Thoughts**

Tax loss harvesting is a legitimate benefit available to investors who are willing to put in the work. But the benefits are modest, so it is not mandatory for a successful retirement plan. We recommend focusing on other things in life unless the opportunity for a one-time large loss presents itself and you are willing to to go through the effort to provide fruit for multiple years.

### **How To Tax Loss Harvest**

  
If you’ve decided the juice is worth the squeeze, follow these steps for better odds of success:

1.  Consult your CPA for any wrinkles that might interfere with your plan, inc. state & local tax exceptions
2.  Check your records for any purchases of the loss asset in the last 30 days **across all your accounts,** inc. automated contributions, grants & dividend reinvestment  (Wait 30 days after a purchase, or accept responsibility for tracking how it offsets the loss)
3.  Turn off any auto-contributions and automated dividend reinvestments in the loss asset in all accounts (or accept responsibility for tracking offsets)
4.  Choose a replacement fund (make sure it’s **not “substantially identical”**)
5.  Identify which specific tax lots you hold at a loss, if not selling an entire position
6.  **During open market hours**, sell the correct tax lots of your TLH asset at a loss, then immediately buy your chosen replacement. (For a mutual fund, request an “exchange” rather than sell+buy.)
7.  Check the trade confirmation to see if the TLH worked: what sale price did you get? Did you achieve a loss?
8.  Do not buy the loss investment again for 30 days after you sell it, **in any account**  
9.  Manually invest any dividend or contribution cash that piles up (across all your accounts) by buying something completely different
10.  Once the wash sale window has closed (day 32 after sale), if the price hasn’t moved much or your replacement is now trading at a loss, consider swapping back to your original holding (you are now tax loss harvesting again)
11.  After the window closes on all tax loss harvesting (day 32 from the last sale), turn back on any automatic contributions, and readjust your dividend reinvestment settings according to your long-term plan
