# 5 Questions To Ask Your Estate Attorney

Estate planning can be a new and unfamiliar topic for many people. It is a broad term that covers several important (though sometimes unpleasant) topics including:  

-   How and to whom your assets will be distributed upon death
-   Who will take care of your minor children if you aren’t able to
-   Who has the power to make financial and healthcare decisions for you
-   Ensuring you have a plan in place for long-term or end-of-life care
-   Minimizing taxes owed by your estate

While there are DIY software options available for simple cases, many people will benefit from using a qualified estate attorney for assistance with their legal documents.

As with hiring any professional, we recommend interviewing at least 2-3 potential candidates so you can get a good sense for different personalities and approaches. But how will you know what to ask and who to pick?

Based on our experience speaking with estate attorneys and clients who have gone through the estate planning process, we have developed this list of 5 questions you can ask your potential attorney to make sure they are a good fit for you.

### **5 Questions To Ask Your Estate Attorney**

**Tell me about your overall process working with a new client. What can I expect?**

Most people who meet with an estate attorney will be doing so for the first time. It is completely understandable to ask the attorney to go over the process with you. In addition to learning about what to expect, you can use this time to develop a sense of the attorney as a person. Are they warm and eager to explain things to you in a very simple, understandable manner? Do you get the sense they would be someone you’re comfortable opening up to and being honest with? Remember, you’ll end up asking this person a lot of questions and telling them some of your most private life details, so make sure you feel like this is someone you will communicate well with.

**Would simple estate documents such as a will and power of attorney be enough for our situation or would we benefit from a more complex solution like a trust?**

Remember that estate planning can range significantly in complexity. If you have very simple needs and limited assets, you might be better off keeping things easy (and lower cost) by using simple tactics like beneficiary designations and getting a will in place. As your situation becomes more complex, more advanced strategies like trusts can be beneficial. If your attorney recommends a trust, they should be able to explain in language that you understand how the process works and what the advantages are for you.

If it helps, a few things that might make a trust more likely to be beneficial are:

-   Owning real estate
-   Having a complex family situation (e.g. divorce, blended families)
-   Wanting a lot of control over the future distribution of your assets
-   Live in a state where probate is expensive or notoriously difficult

**How will you help me on an ongoing basis to ensure actions have been completed and our plans stay up to date?**

Estate planning is not necessarily a one time event. For example, once a trust is created, you have to go through the steps to actually add assets into the trust. Or once your will is created, you may face meaningful life changes like having kids that cause you to rethink your plans. You will want to understand how your potential attorney will help make sure those follow up tasks are completed and how you will work with them over the years to make updates to your plan. A good attorney should give you a sense that they are in your corner for the long haul. Not a one-and-done interaction.

**Can you tell me about your experience with my specific unique situation?**

Some people come into estate planning meetings with unique needs. These might include blended families, living in multiple states, having family overseas, significant wealth, or other highly specific situations. It’s a good idea to ask about the attorney’s expertise in that area. As a follow up, you can also ask them to explain how that unique situation will impact your planning (again, in language you can easily understand). You should come away feeling like you understand the things you will need to think about as a result of your unique circumstances.

**What is your fee structure and what is included in that fee?**

As with all financial matters, it’s important to know the price to make sure you’re getting a good value. Make sure you feel like your potential attorney is being forthcoming with you about all the costs. This means explaining how much you can expect to spend as well as what specific deliverables you will receive. What is the upfront cost? Are there any ongoing or supplemental fees? For example, if you have follow up questions or need to revise the materials in the future, how would they charge for those services?
