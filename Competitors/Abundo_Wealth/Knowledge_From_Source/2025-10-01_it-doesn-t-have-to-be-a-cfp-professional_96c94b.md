# It Doesn't Have to Be a CFP® Professional

When people begin the search for a financial planner, they are thrown into a sea of titles, designations, fee types, and products for sale. After a bit of research, several words start rising to the top. Fiduciary. Fee-Only. Fee-Based. CFP®.

A CFP® professional who is fee-based and a fiduciary. Sounds wonderful, right? Well, not so fast. There are many reasons for continued skepticism, and these terms are not all they’re made out to be.

### **What is a CFP® Professional?**

A CERTIFIED FINANCIAL PLANNER® is someone who has completed the requirements set out by the CFP Board to attain that designation. This includes the “4 E’s”, which stands for Education, Exam, Experience, and Ethics. If you pass the education and exam components, have the requisite 4,000-6,000 hours of experience, sign the ethics attestation, and pay the annual fee you can qualify to become a CFP® professional.

Part of the CFP Board's ethics standard is to act as a fiduciary: placing the client’s interests ahead of their own in all recommendations.

### **The CFP® Certification is not an MD or JD**

Becoming a practicing physician or attorney requires years of grueling education, very difficult testing, and often lengthy periods of continued on-the-job education. After exiting the crucible of the education program, you can add those letters with pride to your name.

The CFP® certification gives the impression of being the financial industry’s version of an MD or JD. **However, there is nothing special a CFP® professional can do that another licensed financial planner cannot do.** All financial advisors must pass regulatory exams - such as the Series 65 exam - to provide investment advice directly to clients. The CFP® certification is a marketable title, not a required license to practice.

The content of the educational curriculum (which can be completed online via taped courses, in some cases) is often geared toward scenarios and products for the ultra-wealthy - spending a lot of time on complex multi-million dollar estate planning techniques, pension plan options for business owners, and the details of different types of expensive life insurance policies - and very little time on the financial issues that actually impact households the most, like debt repayment strategies, budgeting techniques, financial independence, or retirement withdrawal strategies. 

You might also expect the experience requirement to involve a lot of years of direct financial planning and conversations with clients. However, the 6,000 hour experience path (about 3 full years of work) doesn’t actually require providing direct financial planning to clients. It can be fulfilled while doing back office tasks or even writing about financial planning as a journalist.

After completing the process, you have the honor of paying [**$575 per year in fees**](https://www.cfp.net/news/2025/04/new-cfp-certification-fee-effective-october-2025) to the CFP Board, of which an average of $280 goes to their marketing campaigns to push brand awareness. 

The bottom line is there is some useful educational material in process of becoming a CFP® professional, but it is not exactly 6 years of medical school, MCATS, Board Exams, and 4 years of residency.

### **The Person Selling Whole Life Insurance? Yes, They Can Be a CFP® Professional!**

To be clear, being a CFP® professional isn’t a bad thing. It shows a level of commitment to learning and to the profession. It’s just not, in our estimation, the right measuring stick of a good financial planner. Not all CFP® professionals are good advisors, and not all good advisors are CFP® professionals.

We believe the biggest problem is that many CFP® professionals operate with high fee structures and sell expensive products that create clear conflicts of interest. We’ve written many times about the horrible effects of the 1-2% annual fees on your ability to build wealth, and if you look around you’ll find that’s what many CFP® professionals are charging in combined AUM fees and recommended mutual funds fees.

Even worse, many CFP® professionals can and do sell high cost cash-value life insurance and annuity products. Life insurance sales company, Northwestern Mutual, happily shared a press release that they are the [**#1 employer**](https://news.northwesternmutual.com/2024-08-14-Northwestern-Mutual-Leads-Industry-with-Most-Certified-Financial-Planners-in-Prominent-New-Ranking) of CFP® professionals. Unfortunately, the CFP Board doesn’t prohibit these conflicts of interest; it only says advisors must disclose them, which they do beneath pages and pages of often unread disclosures.

We find it hard to believe that high-fee planners are putting their clients' interest ahead of their own when selling high-priced mutual funds, cash-value insurance policies, or investment annuities.

### **Fiduciary, Fee-Only, and Fee-Based Don’t Mean What You Think**

Costs matter when choosing an advisor! But the industry has created confusion for clients with its many similar terms. Fee-only, fee-based, and fiduciary are not terms you can reliably use to separate good financial planners from bad ones or cheap ones from expensive ones. Instead, pay attention to the total cost and the payment model.

We hear a lot of clients say (with great intentions) that they’ve heard that a “**fee-based**” advisor is best. But fee-based means the advisor can charge both investment management fees AND commissions on product sales! We see that as a conflict of interest, period. We recommend avoiding relationships where your advisor profits off of the sales commission from something they sell you.

The slightly better version of fee-based is **fee-only**, but this only means no commissions. It doesn’t mean low fees! Someone could charge you a 2% AUM (Assets Under Management) fee and correctly state that they are “fee-only”. It means the fees you pay are independent of the products they place you in, not that the fees are fair, low, or advantageous to you.

The most difficult term to pin down is “**fiduciary**.” We agree that you want your advisor to place your interests ahead of their own when getting advice, but we believe the best way to do that is with the correct incentive structure of flat fee, advice-only planning. If your advisor profits from having more assets under management, will they recommend you pay off your mortgage early? Maybe, but they have to fight their own interests to do so. It may be no wonder then that so many AUM advisors are desperate for you to roll over your 401(k) to an IRA or take the lump sum pension payout. That may or may not be the right idea for you, but it is always a win for their advisor’s fees!

### **You Can Be a Great Planner Without a CFP® Certification**

Because of the CFP Board’s successful marketing campaign, many people are convinced they should work with a planner with the credentials. But as we’ve discussed, that measuring stick can land you in a high-fee, conflict-of-interest laden relationship.

We know a great many planners, including several at Abundo, who are not CFP® professionals. Those who commit to learning deeply about the topics most relevant to their clients and to educating others can be highly successful advisors. And remember, to be a financial advisor in the first place means passing the same licensing requirements regardless of CFP® professional status.

In fact, we think career changers (including those who never acquire a CFP® certification) are often outstanding financial planners because they didn’t grow up steeped in the sales-heavy nature of the industry. They saw financial planning from the outside, from the position of clients, and can help guide people to the right behavioral solutions, simple explanations, and low-cost tools to get the job done right.

At the end of the day, it isn’t the letters behind your name that make you a great advisor. It’s your skills and the value you bring to clients. Rather than focusing on only looking for CFP® professionals, we encourage you to meet with your prospective advisor and get to know them, their level of expertise, and how well they understand what you’re looking for. In the discussion, also ask detailed questions about how the advisor is paid - including all their sources of revenue - to ensure their incentives align with your best interests.

We hope that you'll be in much better hands that way.

### **Conclusion**

We respect the decision to work only with someone who has the CFP® certification, but we don’t recommend relying on it as a key filter when choosing an advisor. Instead, focus on whether your advisor’s compensation and approach are truly aligned with your best interests. Look for someone with a transparent, low-fee structure, along with the education and experience relevant to the type of planning you need.

At Abundo, we have built our firm around the [**advice-only**](https://www.abundowealth.com/blog/what-is-a-flat-fee-financial-advisor), flat-fee model because we strongly believe this is the best way to reduce conflicts of interests, limit costs, and deliver high-quality advice that helps clients achieve their goals.
