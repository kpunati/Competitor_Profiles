# My CPA Coach — Summary

> Online tax-strategy and CPA-services firm led by **Sherman & Krystal Standberry (CPAs)** — explicitly tier-priced ($5,000 / $7,500 / $10,000 first-year tax planning, with year-2+ maintenance pricing also published), with a defined money-back guarantee mechanic, eight named-with-credentials testimonials, an active long-tail-SEO blog, and a self-paced "Tax Savers Course" product running on a separate `learn.mycpacoach.com` LMS subdomain. Targets small-business owners and high-income earners.

## The headline

My CPA Coach is the **most disciplined fee-transparent operator in the tax-strategy bucket** of our catalog. Where Tax Alchemy publishes a $20K–$150K *band* and KDA Inc. gates pricing entirely, My CPA Coach publishes a clean **three-tier table with specific dollar amounts** (Comprehensive $5K / Premium $7.5K / Elite $10K, first year) AND a separate maintenance-pricing table for year 2+. The guarantee mechanic is also more carefully drafted than Tax Alchemy's: refund up to 100% of the planning fee if (a) the plan doesn't identify ≥2x the fee in estimated tax savings, (b) the business earns >$100K in revenue, and (c) the client provided all required information. The disclosure section reads like it was reviewed by counsel.

The firm targets a customer who looks structurally identical to Tax Alchemy's but at one-third the price point — small-business owners and high-income earners with complex tax situations but who haven't yet escalated to "ultra-affluent." Threat to our retail RIA serving early-adults and 40-50 401(k) holders is **low** because the product is tax planning, not wealth management — different decision tree.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **The cleanest published-tier pricing in the tax bucket of our catalog.** Three named tiers, specific dollar prices, separate Year-1 vs Year-2+ tables, feature deltas across tiers shown as info-tooltips on the pricing page. Compare: Tax Alchemy publishes a band ($20K–$150K) without internal tier deltas. KDA Inc. gates entirely. My CPA Coach publishes everything.
- **The named-with-last-initial testimonials.** "Alex H.", "Ted K.", "Wakila S.", "Kenn R.", "Brandon M." — first-name + last-initial is the strongest attribution most tax-firm clients will permit, and My CPA Coach uses it consistently across all five visible testimonials (plus three duplicates in the homepage carousel). Compare to Tax Alchemy (zero named testimonials).
- **The income-bracket prospect-qualifier on the lead form.** Seven brackets from <$50K to >$5M. This pre-qualifies *the firm's own time* — the form is the screening tool, not the sales pitch.
- **A defensible guarantee mechanic.** Three required conditions to invoke (savings <2x fee, $100K+ revenue, all info provided) make the guarantee meaningful without making it abusable. Counsel-reviewed-looking disclosure.
- **A clean tier ladder with separate Year-2 maintenance pricing.** Telegraphs that the firm thinks about retention as a separate commercial event, not just initial close. Year 2+ maintenance pricing is unusual to publish — most professional-services firms hide ongoing fees inside the consultation pitch.
- **A standalone LMS product** (`learn.mycpacoach.com`) at the bottom of the price ladder — "save money and enroll into our self-guided course to learn how to reduce your own taxes." This is the cheap-to-deliver layer of the product stack that converts the prospects who *can't* (or *won't*) afford the $5K planning tier into customers anyway.
- **A blog with long-tail-SEO titles.** "34 Big Tax Deductions (Write-offs) for Businesses in 2026" reads like keyword-research output, which means it'll rank.
- **Spouse-and-CPA founder pairing.** Sherman & Krystal Standberry as a CPA-and-business-partner couple is unusual in the category and gives the firm a relatability angle that Tax Alchemy's "Karlton + anonymous team" framing doesn't have. Multiple homepage testimonials reference "this wonderful couple."

## Where they're vulnerable

- **No published physical address.** "Online CPA services" is the framing, but for a fee-transparent firm with named principals, the absence of a state of operation (which determines the state Board of Accountancy that licenses Sherman) is a real-trust gap.
- **"Featured In" banner without logo callouts in captured copy.** The credibility claim is asserted but not specified. A prospect can't tell whether "Featured In" means Forbes or a tax-industry newsletter.
- **No team photos / headshots captured.** The named team is text-only on captured pages (the about-page meta description names four people, but the page itself wasn't fully crawled).
- **Money-back guarantee invites the same regulatory question as Tax Alchemy's** — state Boards of Accountancy restrict outcome guarantees; the qualifiers ($100K revenue, all info provided) help but a state board could still object.
- **The income-bracket form goes up to >$5M** but the pricing tier-ladder caps at $10K (Elite). A prospect earning $5M+ would expect a higher tier than the Elite plan offers — the firm is positioned for the mid-market, but the form acquires high-end prospects who probably need to be referred onward or hand-handled.
- **Key-person risk on Sherman.** CPA-licensed founder + named throughout the brand. If he steps back, the licensing posture changes.

## What this means for our entry

My CPA Coach is **not on our threat radar** for direct prospect competition — but it's the **single best fee-transparency operator** in the tax-strategy bucket of our catalog. The structural lessons:

1. **Publish the actual prices, not a band.** Tax Alchemy publishes $20K–$150K; My CPA Coach publishes $5,000 / $7,500 / $10,000. The latter is more powerful because a prospect can self-select to the right tier before the sales call. For our RIA, this would mean a specific dollar fee for our two segment-specific plans — not a "starting at" or a range.
2. **Publish year-2+ maintenance pricing.** Most professional services hide ongoing fees. Publishing them telegraphs durability and lets prospects evaluate lifetime cost up-front.
3. **Use income-bracket qualifiers on the lead form.** A 7-bracket "annual income" field is the cheapest pre-qualifier in the catalog and tells the firm whether to route to junior or senior staff before the call.
4. **Use first-name + last-initial testimonials.** It's the strongest attribution most retail clients will grant. PWL and Ritholtz do better (named + photos), but for an SMB-focused firm this is plenty.
5. **Build a course / LMS product at the bottom of the price ladder.** $5K is too expensive for the prospect who's not yet ready; "a few hundred dollars" for self-paced learning captures that prospect's wallet and email at the same time, and primes future upgrade. For us this could be a $99 / year "Set up your first investment account" companion.

What we should explicitly *not* copy: the absence of physical address (we need to publish ours), the asserted-but-not-specified "Featured In" badge (we should name the publication or omit the badge), and the guarantee mechanic (different regulatory regime — SEC Marketing Rule prohibits anything that smells like a return guarantee).

The narrowest single takeaway: **the My CPA Coach pricing page is the single most useful artifact to mirror in our own pricing-page wireframes** — clearer than Tax Alchemy's band, more specific than Intrepid's percentage tiers, and with an explicit Year-2+ table that makes lifetime cost legible.
