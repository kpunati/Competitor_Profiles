# Foundry Financial — Summary

> A small Pasadena, CA fee-based RIA explicitly built around one ICP — retirees and pre-retirees with tax-burdened portfolios — selling comprehensive wealth management via a trademarked "Foundry Framework" and a single "Are We a Good Fit?" retirement-assessment funnel.

## The headline

Foundry Financial is what a *disciplined single-segment boutique* looks like in 2026: clean modern design, a clearly named target client ("diligent savers nearing retirement with a significant tax burden"), one CTA on every page, and a founder origin story that justifies the firm's existence. It's a low-threat competitor to us — they aim past our two target demographics by 15–20 years — but it's a useful study in segment discipline, ICP clarity, and conversion focus that we should absolutely steal from. The `.org` TLD turns out to be a marketing-domain choice, not a non-profit or foundation structure; the entity is a standard SEC-registered LLC.

## What they sell, to whom

Comprehensive wealth management (retirement income, tax planning, evidence-based portfolio design, estate planning, risk management) bundled into a single offering, sold to retirees and pre-retirees who have accumulated meaningful assets and are facing tax-bracket optimization questions in decumulation. Pricing is gated — the About page asserts "transparent pricing" but no fee schedule appears on captured site pages. Buyer onboarding is a single conversion path: every CTA on every page links to `/retirement-assessment` ("Are We a Good Fit?" → free strategy meeting). The average account size is roughly $370K (AUM ÷ accounts), placing the firm in the upper mass-affluent / lower-HNW tier.

## How big a threat

**Low.** Foundry serves a different demographic than either of our target segments (early adults; mid-career 401(k) holders), runs a sparse-and-bursty blog rather than a content engine, has no national reach, and shows no momentum signals in captured source data. Four-axis rubric: Reach 1 (single Pasadena office, dormant blog, light press footprint), Segment overlap 1 (explicitly retirees only), Momentum 1 (no visible growth signals; nine blog posts over six years), Capital backing 2 (independent profitable boutique) → sum 5 → low. They aren't competing for our prospects today; they are competing for retirees who could otherwise hire Walser or any number of local LA-area retirement-focused RIAs.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Single explicit ICP sentence on the About page** — "retirees and those nearing retirement who have been diligent savers, have little debt and are facing a significant tax burden in retirement" — does more positioning work than most firms' entire homepages.
- **One CTA, one destination, every page.** Every conversion link goes to `/retirement-assessment`. No competing offers, no chat widget, no "contact us" fallback. Conversion-disciplined design.
- **Founder origin story as trust mechanism.** "When my dad died entirely too early, I tried to help my Mom find an advisor — instead I found an industry of glorified salespeople." Best single passage on the site.
- **A trademarked named process — Foundry Framework™.** Small branding move that lets the firm refer to its approach as a *thing* rather than a generic service.
- **Modern visual identity:** black-and-white photography, single cyan accent (`#00A1E1`), Lato headings, generous spacing. Looks like a designer made it, not a Wix template.
- **The "Are We a Good Fit?" CTA framing** is softer than "Schedule a Call" but pre-qualifies the prospect's mindset in a way harder asks don't.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **"Transparent pricing" asserted but not shown.** No fee schedule on any captured page despite the About-page claim. Easy credibility gap to exploit.
- **No visible team.** 7 advisors and no advisor photos/bios on the captured pages; a team page URL exists in nav but wasn't pulled by sitemap-first discovery. Hiding the team contradicts the "real humans, no salespeople" pitch.
- **Sparse blog cadence.** Roughly nine posts over six years; multi-year gaps. The site reads partly dormant.
- **Single-segment dependency.** No funnel for any client who isn't already nearing retirement. As the existing book ages out, there's no replenishment engine.
- **Modern-tech claim is asserted, not demonstrated.** Site references a "financial dashboard" but shows no portal, no screenshots, no demo. Standard category gap.
- **Key-person risk.** First-person About page narrative; the firm is the founder's story.

## What this means for our entry

Foundry isn't a direct competitor for the prospects we care about — but they are a useful **structural model** for how a single-segment RIA wins its segment. The lessons we should steal: write the explicit ICP sentence, commit to one CTA, ground the firm in a founder origin paragraph, name our process. The lessons we should ignore: their single-segment scope, their hidden team, their asserted-but-undemonstrated pricing transparency, and their bursty blog. The real entry openings against Foundry specifically are (a) the early-adult and mid-career segments they ignore by design, and (b) the modern-tech claim they assert but don't show — exactly the axis our thesis says we should own.
