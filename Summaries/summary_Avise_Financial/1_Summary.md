# Avise Financial — Summary

> **Self-described "RIA co-op"** — a member-owned platform of independent CFP®-led advisor practices. Co-founded by **Leighann Miko, CFP®** (CSU Northridge → prior independent RIA) and **Katrina Soelter, CFP®** (UC Berkeley). **$169.4M aggregated AUM across 6 member advisors, 7 employees, 0 DRPs.** Revenue-share fee model: **15% / 10% / 5% banded** by advisor revenue. Provides operations + tech + compliance + outsourced support ($45-$70/hr) to member advisors; runs a consumer-facing "Match With An Advisor" directory on the front-end. Five stated values: Value-Aligned · Independent · Personal · Diverse · Transparent.

## The headline

Avise is structurally **closer to [XY Planning Network](../Xy_Planning_Network/1_Summary.md) than to any other firm in this batch** — a B2B platform that supports independent advisors plus a consumer-facing matching directory. What makes it distinct: it operates as a **cooperative**, with member ownership, democratic voting rights, and patronage dividends distributed from annual profits. The 15% / 10% / 5% revenue-share is the platform's compensation model — member advisors pay a tiered cut of their revenue, with marginal rates that decrease as the advisor's book grows.

This is *not* a direct competitor to USWM at the consumer level. It's a competitor to USWM's *go-it-alone* path — the alternative USWM could have chosen instead of building independently. Worth knowing about as a structural option (USWM could potentially join a network like this if scale-up gets hard), but not on the threat radar for client acquisition.

## What they sell, to whom

**Two sides of the platform:**

### Advisor-facing (B2B)

Member advisors get:
- **Operations**: compliance, regulatory outsourcing, billing, performance reporting, insurance
- **Tech suite**: AdvicePay · Advyzon · MessageWatcher · Wealthbox
- **Outsourced support** (hourly): client onboarding, financial planning, investment research at **$45–$70/hr**
- **Governance**: democratic voting + patronage dividends

**Fee structure** (tiered revenue share):
| Advisor revenue band | Avise share |
|---|---|
| $0–$500K | 15% |
| $500K–$1M | 10% |
| $1M+ | 5% |

### Consumer-facing

The "Match With An Advisor" page is a questionnaire-to-match flow: prospect fills out a short questionnaire, Avise introduces them to one of the member advisors. Free to the consumer. End-client fee arrangements happen at the member-advisor level (not standardized at Avise's layer).

**Positioning copy**: *"Genuine financial advice for genuine people."* · *"Avise is a collective that helps good-hearted financial advisors start and operate their own independent firms and work with more people like you."* · *"Financial corporations spend hundreds of millions on advertising to capture your attention."* — explicit anti-corporate-finance framing.

## How big a threat

**Low.** **Reach 1** ($169M aggregated, recent registration, modest brand reach), **Segment overlap 1** (B2B-primary; consumer side competes for any advice-seeking prospect, but match-flow is generic, not segment-targeted), **Momentum 2** (recent founding + cooperative model is novel + 6 member advisors growing), **Capital backing 1** (no external investment by design — cooperative) → sum **5 → low**.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Cooperative governance as differentiation.** Most advisor networks (XYPN, Focus, etc.) are corporate platforms. Avise's "RIA co-op" framing — democratic voting + patronage dividends — is genuinely novel and aligns advisor incentives with platform success in a way corporate platforms can't credibly replicate.
- **Banded revenue share that rewards scale.** 15% → 10% → 5% means as advisors grow they keep more. This is structurally pro-advisor in a way that flat-percentage platforms aren't.
- **Outsourced ops at published hourly rates ($45-$70).** Concrete cost discovery for member-advisor consideration. Most platforms gate this behind a sales call.
- **Explicit anti-corporate-finance positioning.** "Financial corporations spend hundreds of millions on advertising to capture your attention" is direct rhetoric on the homepage — same vibe as Cordant's "Wall Street bank" framing.
- **Founder credentials matter.** Both co-founders are CFP®-credentialed practitioners, not pure tech / VC founders. The platform is built by advisors for advisors, which gives it more credibility than the corporate alternatives.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **Consumer funnel competes with member firms.** "Match With An Advisor" sends prospects to ONE matched advisor — but Avise is the front of the funnel, not the advisor. If a member firm builds its own brand strong enough, the prospect bypasses Avise. Network economics fragile.
- **Small aggregated AUM ($169M / 6 advisors = ~$28M per advisor)**. That's tiny per-head; the math has to scale for the platform model to support its overhead.
- **Co-op governance complexity.** Democratic voting works at small scale, but the model gets harder to govern as membership grows. XYPN avoids this by being corporate.
- **Brand reach modest.** Without a personality at the front (no Carl Richards / Michael Kitces equivalent), the platform's outbound is limited to founder-led + member referrals.

## What this means for our entry

Avise is **not on USWM's direct threat radar** — it's a structural alternative to USWM's independent path, not a competitor for the same end-clients. Strategic value:

1. **Co-op as a future structural option.** If USWM scales to where the founder-led model strains (say, 5+ advisors), joining or building a co-op like Avise is a credible alternative to selling to a roll-up like Mercer Advisors or Focus Partners. The cooperative model preserves independence + ownership in a way M&A doesn't.
2. **Hourly outsourced rates as a benchmark.** $45-$70/hr for outsourced financial planning + investment research tells USWM what the *floor cost* of those services looks like.
3. **CFP + CFP founder pairing.** Two CFP®-credentialed co-founders building together is a positive signal — but Avise's positioning leans on "two CFPs starting a co-op" without the kind of irreducible prior-career arc that Kean (wirehouse-to-independent) brings. USWM has stronger origin material than Avise.
4. **Anti-corporate-finance framing.** Avise echoes Cordant's "Wall Street bank" rhetoric. USWM should claim space in this pattern via Kean's actual wirehouse departure story.

The narrowest single takeaway: **the cooperative model is worth understanding now in case USWM's growth path eventually warrants joining one** — but for daily competitive positioning, Avise is mostly a B2B story.
