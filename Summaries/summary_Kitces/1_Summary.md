# Kitces.com — Summary

> A B2B research, education, and CE-credit publication for financial advisors, built around Michael Kitces — not a wealth-management firm and not a competitor for our retail prospects.

## The headline

Kitces.com is the industry's most influential trade publication for fee-only RIAs and CFPs — 78,507 advisor newsletter subscribers, 488 podcast episodes, ~578 pages of blog archive — and it does not address retail wealth-management prospects at all. The right way to think about Kitces is as a *category-shaping* peer-press input we should read religiously rather than a head-on competitor. Almost every framing the rest of our peer-RIA set uses (fee structures, niching, AdvisorTech adoption, the post-AI value of human advice) traces back to something Kitces published first.

## Who they are

- **Founded**: (not in captured data — blog widely dated to ~2008 in industry context)
- **Size**: ~5 named contributors visible in bylines; private/founder-owned publication business
- **Where they're based**: (not in captured data — no physical address on captured pages)
- **How they're funded**: subscription revenue (The Kitces Report), CE course revenue, event revenue, plus Michael's affiliated speaking and consulting. Editorial-independence promise: *"We do NOT sell/share any data with advertisers & permit NO third-party marketing."*

## What they sell, to whom

To **financial advisors, advisor firms, and AdvisorTech vendors** — not to retail wealth clients. The stack: free blog (Nerd's Eye View), free newsletter with a free-report lead magnet ("Quantifying the Value of Financial Planning Advice"), paid subscription research (The Kitces Report — AdvisorTech / FP Productivity / Marketing / Wellbeing verticals; Individual + Group tiers), paid CE courses for CFP/IMCA/IAR credit, live events (Kitces Marketing Summit 2026 on June 4), two podcasts (FA Success, Kitces & Carl), Michael's speaking and consulting. Subscription pricing is gated; the funnel is email-first.

## How big a threat

**Low** for our prospect-acquisition motion. Kitces sells to advisors, not consumers — the "FOR CONSUMERS" link on the site is a single referral page, not an acquisition surface. Our two target segments (early-adult first-time investors and 40–50 year old 401(k)-focused mid-career) are not addressed.

The real influence is *categorical, not transactional*. Kitces shapes how every peer fee-only RIA in our competitive set thinks about positioning, pricing, niching, and marketing. The frames our messaging will be benchmarked against by industry observers — "advicer" vs. "advisor," fee-only fiduciary norms, the value of comprehensive planning, AI-and-the-human-element — have all been published on this site first. We do not compete with Kitces; we operate inside a category narrative he co-authored. The correct posture is regular consumption, not engagement-as-rival. *(Michael also co-founded XY Planning Network, the B2B backbone for new fee-only RIAs serving Gen X/Y clients — that ecosystem is far more directly relevant to our business model and deserves its own profile.)*

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **A single distinctive vocabulary word ("advicer") doing positioning work everywhere** — the relabeling is a one-word manifesto, used in nav, hero copy, audience-count claims, and the "Advicer Manifesto" footer link.
- **Category-defining lead magnet.** "Quantifying the Value of Financial Planning Advice" is a research-shaped title that earns an email signup the way a generic "5 Tips" PDF never would.
- **Email-first funnel.** The primary CTA across the page is *"GET BLOG UPDATES!"* — give us your email is the conversion. Paid product sits behind that.
- **Named bylines on every post.** Real humans, photos, multi-year archive depth. Stark contrast with the "our team writes for you" anonymity that dominates wealth-firm content.
- **Editorial-independence promise as trust signal.** *"We do NOT sell/share any data with advertisers."* In a category full of vendor-sponsored content, that copy is a moat.
- **Omnichannel distribution, one voice.** Blog + two podcasts + newsletter + research reports + CE courses + live events + speaking circuit — all unmistakably Michael's voice, all one brand.

## Where they're vulnerable

These are *category gaps* a focused entrant could fill, not direct competitive weaknesses:

- **No consumer-facing content of comparable depth under the Kitces brand.** The "FOR CONSUMERS" path is two cosmetic nav items pointing at one referral page. A consumer-RIA writing at Kitces-grade rigor for actual prospects is operating in white space.
- **Visual identity is dated by 2026 standards** — three-blues-plus-olive-green palette, ubiquitous Lato at 400, dense 2010s-WordPress blog feed. Durable for an established publication; unmissable as "older internet" against modern brands.
- **Vocabulary is sealed inside the profession.** "Advicer," "NUA," "Monte Carlo retirement projection," "AdvisorTech" — alienating to retail prospects. A firm that takes Kitces's *substance* and translates the *language* has an opening.
- **No transparent pricing on any paid product** — fine for a B2B research subscription, but not a model to copy for consumer-facing services.

## What this means for our entry

Treat Kitces as **required reading, not as a competitor**. Subscribe to the newsletter and FA Success podcast, pull the "Quantifying the Value of Financial Planning Advice" report, and read his AdvisorTech column monthly — the frames published there will be the frames every industry observer uses to evaluate our positioning. The one tactical takeaway worth borrowing on our consumer-facing site is the *named-byline, email-first, research-shaped-lead-magnet* funnel pattern, translated into plain-English consumer language. The strategic note worth investigating separately is XY Planning Network, the adjacent venture far more directly relevant to our business model.
