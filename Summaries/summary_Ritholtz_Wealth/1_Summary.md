# Ritholtz Wealth Management — Summary

> An employee-owned, content-marketed RIA built around the public personalities of Barry Ritholtz and Josh Brown — and the most direct competitor we've profiled to date.

## The headline

Ritholtz is what wealth management looks like when the brand *is* the product. Fifteen years of public-facing content (blogs, podcasts, "The Compound" media network, TV appearances) built an audience that converts into clients without traditional marketing spend. They're employee-owned (a direct jab at PE-backed roll-ups like Focus Partners), they publish tier-by-tier pricing on the home page (radical transparency for the category), and they serve every wealth level from $0 (via the Liftoff robo and the "Good Advice" tier) up to multi-family-office. Two things matter for us: they directly serve both of our target segments, and they share our planned "true independence" claim — which means we cannot win on independence alone.

## Who they are

- **AUM**: **$7.70 billion** as of 2026-03-02 (ADV Item 5.F.2.c). Roughly 4% of Focus Partners' scale, with a fraction of the headcount — they punch *above* their AUM weight through media reach.
- **Size**: 83 total employees / 48 advisory-function employees / 40 branches
- **Founded**: ~15 years ago per their About copy (verify via ADV)
- **Where they're based**: 24 West 40th Street, 15th Floor, New York, NY
- **How they're funded**: **100% employee-owned. No outside investors.** Stated explicitly on the About page as a direct contrast vs. PE-backed RIA aggregators.

## What they sell, to whom

Four AUM-tiered packages published on the homepage: **Good Advice** ($0–1M, entry tier with shared advisor team), **Flagship** ($1M–7M, dedicated advisor), **The Preserve** ($7M–25M, legacy-focused), **Multi-Family Office** ($25M+, concierge). Plus a separately branded robo-advisor (**Liftoff**) for the lower end, and dedicated service lines for tax, estate, insurance, equity compensation, corporate retirement (401(k) plans), and institutional investing. The buyer enters through content — read the blog, listen to The Compound, book a call — not through paid acquisition.

## How big a threat

**High.** Strongest direct competitor we've profiled. They are credibly serving both of our target segments (Good Advice + Liftoff covers early adults; Flagship + Corporate Retirement covers the 40–50yo 401(k) cohort), they share our planned "independent / employee-owned" positioning, and they have a fifteen-year content moat we cannot match with any reasonable timeline. The good news: their strongest moats are exactly the things we don't have to compete on directly — their personality-led media engine and their veteran public profile. The actionable threat is what we'll need to clearly differentiate against, not match.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Published tier-by-tier pricing on the home page.** Eliminates the usual "schedule a call to learn pricing" friction. Proves fee transparency is commercially viable in this category — it's not just a hypothesis.
- **Named, bylined, public-facing advisors.** Homepage shows 15+ recent blog posts each with a specific advisor's byline (Barry Ritholtz, Josh Brown, Ben Carlson, Tony Isola, Blair duQuesnay, etc.). Trust is built through evidence of voice, not stock photography.
- **"True independence" framing as a competitive jab.** One paragraph on the About page does enormous positioning work: "We have no outside investors. […] While other RIA founders run around the country collecting money from private equity firms, we remain truly independent." Specific, pointed, easy to remember.
- **Sub-brands for tiered products.** "Good Advice," "Liftoff," "The Preserve" — each tier is named like a product, not labeled like a level.
- **Voice with a point of view.** "WE FOCUS ON WHAT WORKS. NO HYPE. NO SHORTCUTS." reads more like a tee-shirt than a tagline. They've decided what they think and they're saying it.
- **Bigger typography, brighter palette, yellow CTA.** Visually signals "not your father's wealth firm" without losing seriousness.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **Moat depends on a small number of personal brands.** Barry and Josh do most of the public-facing work. If either steps back, the recruiting story ("everyone here found us through the message") gets harder to maintain.
- **Voice is aimed at one kind of client.** Confident, slightly swaggering — works great for a 32-year-old engineer, lands worse for a 52-year-old corporate executive thinking about retirement. They're under-aimed at mid-career retirement-focused clients despite having the tier products for them.
- **No visible client-tech story.** For a firm that calls itself "cutting edge," the site shows zero client portal screenshots, planning software, dashboards, or "what working with us actually looks like." The "modern" claim is delivered through tone and content, not through visible product. This is the single biggest gap a modern-tooling-forward entrant can exploit.
- **Content engine is pointed at sophisticated investors, not the demographics they serve.** Good Advice and Liftoff exist for early adults but their content output (alpha, behavioral investing, market commentary) isn't aimed at someone with $20K in their first brokerage. A firm that produces content *for* early-career and *for* mid-career retirement specifically can win prospects who feel directly addressed.

## What this means for our entry

Ritholtz forces us to be honest about our edge. We cannot win on "independence" — they own it. We cannot win on "content volume" — they have a fifteen-year head start. We cannot win on "personality-led brand" — we don't have Barry Ritholtz on our team. Our actual edge is the one they conspicuously aren't showing on their site: **the modern client-facing tech experience.** Lead with a visible product — dashboards, planning software, portal screenshots — that makes Ritholtz's site feel like description while ours looks like demonstration. Pair that with segment-specific landing pages (one for early adults, one for 40–50yo 401(k) focus) that speak directly to a life stage in a way Ritholtz's homogeneous voice doesn't. And steal the one move we can credibly copy: **publish pricing tiers on day one.**
