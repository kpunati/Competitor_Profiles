# ClearValue Investing — Summary

> **Subscription investing-education membership site** — Free tier + **Premium $14.99/month ($180/yr)** — operated by "the ClearValue Team" (no individuals named on captured pages). Stock and crypto picks, technical analysis, options, index-fund coverage, community message board, pre-and-post-market briefings, and a live market ticker on every page. Carefully scoped as **education, not advice** — the FAQ explicitly disclaims trade execution and personalized recommendations, which is the standard regulatory posture that keeps a content publisher outside SEC RIA registration.

## The headline

ClearValue Investing is what a **modern paid-newsletter / investing-membership site** looks like in 2026 — built on the same legal foundations as Motley Fool, Stansberry Research, and the Substack-publisher constellation that has reshaped retail investing content. The mechanics are tight: a free tier that captures email + community membership, a paid tier ($14.99/mo) that unlocks team-curated stock picks and technical analysis, a live market-data ticker that makes the site feel daily-relevant, and a news-aggregation feed on the homepage that surfaces ~15 financial-news headlines from external sources. **No advisor is named.** No team page is captured. No specific picks are visible without subscribing. The legal scaffolding — visible in the FAQ that explicitly addresses "Do you assist with making trades?" and "Are you able to offer personalized recommendations?" — is the careful-by-design posture that lets the firm publish stock picks without RIA registration.

Threat to our retail RIA is **low**. The target customer is a self-directed retail investor in the 25-45 range who wants stock-pick ideas plus a community, not a wealth manager. The audience overlap with our two target segments is real (early-adult investors are exactly the prospect demographic) but the *product overlap is zero* — we sell allocation + planning + management; ClearValue sells picks + community. **Same prospect, different decision.**

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **The $14.99/mo entry point.** Cheap enough to be a casual yes; expensive enough to filter for someone willing to pay for investing content. This price point has been the durable winner across the paid-newsletter category (Motley Fool Stock Advisor was $99/yr for years; Stratechery is $12/mo; Substack publishers typically $10–$30/mo). ClearValue is correctly anchored.
- **A live market-data ticker on every page.** Free, fresh-feeling, gives the site a daily-news-property feel rather than a static-content-site feel. The cost of running this is real but the perceived liveness is worth it.
- **News-aggregation feed on the homepage.** ~15 external financial headlines surfaced in real-time. Cheap acquisition cost (RSS / API) and high signal of "this site is paying attention to the market every day."
- **A clearly published two-tier pricing structure.** Compare to the gated-pricing firms in our catalog (Foundry, KDA Inc., Streamline, Synergy Private). ClearValue Investing is **fee-transparent** — small thing, but a small advantage at the prospect's first impression.
- **The legal-scaffolding FAQ.** Explicitly addressing "Do you assist with making trades?" and "Can you provide assurances regarding performance of investments?" is the kind of explicit regulatory hygiene that smaller paid-newsletter sites skip. Whoever drafted this site read the SEC RIA-perimeter cases.
- **A community message board** as a Premium feature. Substack publishers have proven that *the community is the moat* in paid-newsletter business — once a member is engaged with other members, churn drops. ClearValue is using the same mechanic.
- **GDPR-compliant cookie-consent flow** on the homepage. Suggests either real European traffic or modern-WP-template defaults. Either way, signals professionalism.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **No team named on captured pages.** "The ClearValue Team" is generic. Compare to Sven Carlin (full bio + PhD), Karlton Dennis (face of the brand), Kitces (Michael Kitces personally) — ClearValue's anonymity is by design, possibly, but it undercuts trust for higher-stakes investing content.
- **No track-record claim captured.** No "X-year track record," no "X% average annual return," no "outperformed S&P by X%." For a paid-pick-publishing site, the absence of a track-record claim is conspicuous (paid-newsletter regulations also restrict performance claims — possibly intentional avoidance).
- **No physical address.** Modern paid-newsletter sites often disclose a state-of-operation or registered office; ClearValue doesn't.
- **Anonymized testimonials.** Single "Michael Bryant" quote captured. No firm affiliation, no photo, no LinkedIn. Compare to Asset Map's 8 named-CFP-and-firm testimonials, or My CPA Coach's 5 first-name-last-initial customers.
- **No early-adult / first-investor surface.** The site assumes the visitor *already wants stock picks*, which prices out the early-adult "I don't know where to start" prospect we care about. Implicit barrier to entry.
- **Brand confusability.** "ClearValue Investing" vs "ClearValue Tax" — whether or not these are the same operator, the brand-architecture isn't disambiguated on the captured site. A prospect can't tell if they're getting the same team.
- **No 401(k) / retirement-planning content visible** in captured top-level structure. The product is stock-picking, not retirement-allocation.

## What this means for our entry

ClearValue Investing is **not on our threat radar** for direct prospect competition — different product, different decision tree. Strategic value:

1. **The $14.99/mo subscription price point** is the dominant anchor in the retail-investing-content space. We are *not* a content business, but if we ever ship a paid-content surface alongside the RIA, this is the price point to remember.
2. **The live market-data ticker + news-aggregation feed pattern** is a useful daily-relevance mechanic. Most RIA sites in our catalog look like brochureware; a small live-data widget would make ours feel current. Worth a wireframe.
3. **The "education, not advice" legal scaffolding** is the standard playbook for any content-publishing surface we might run that includes specific instrument names. We should write our own FAQ in that mold — "Do you make personalized recommendations? (No, this content is general education — your specific situation requires an advisor conversation.)" — to keep our content surface clean of inadvertent-advice risk.
4. **The Premium-tier community-message-board pattern** is the right paid-newsletter retention mechanic. Not directly applicable to our RIA, but the lesson — *community is the moat* — transfers to any paid content we ship.

What we should explicitly *not* copy: the anonymous team, the absence of physical address, the no-track-record claim style (which protects them legally but undercuts conversion), the brand-architecture ambiguity with whatever-else-is-named-ClearValue, and the picks-not-planning product framing.

The narrowest single takeaway: **ClearValue's $14.99/mo + free-tier + community model is the cleanest paid-newsletter execution in our catalog** — useful structural reference if we ever launch a content surface, but not a competitor we will encounter at the wealth-management decision point.
