# Roadmap Money — Summary

> Personal-finance blog + affiliate-marketing site by **Jacob Wade** — explicitly NOT a personal-finance professional per his own site disclaimer. Content surfaces: budgeting templates · save-money guides · debt-payoff (snowball spreadsheet) · investing-app reviews · credit-card affiliate offers · life-insurance comparison · Coast FIRE calculator (lead magnet). Featured-on deck: CBS News · MSN Money · Forbes · Nasdaq · Yahoo! Finance · Go Banking Rates · AOL Finance. **Content appears stale** — most-popular posts dated 2020-2022; copyright "©2023."

## The headline

Roadmap Money is the **clearest example in our catalog of the "personal-finance affiliate blog" archetype** — a content site monetized through credit-card / investing-app / life-insurance affiliate partnerships rather than advisory fees or course sales. Jacob is a real personal-finance writer (bylines at The Balance and Investing Answers; mainstream-press mentions) but the site itself is mid-life-cycle: heavy SEO-friendly product-review content from 2020-2022, lighter recent updates. Site disclaimer is unambiguous: *"I am not a personal finance professional."*

Threat to our retail RIA is **low** — same regulatory carve-out shape as ClearValue Investing, Sven Carlin's blog, and the marketing portion of Heritage Wealth Planning. Audience-attention competition with the early-adult segment (someone Googling "best investing app for beginners" might land here instead of finding a wealth advisor), but no advisory-product competition.

The site is in *income-property mode* — running on existing organic traffic, generating affiliate commissions, with limited new content production.

## How big a threat

**Low.** Four-axis rubric: **Reach 2** (mainstream-press feature deck is real but consumer-traffic estimates not available; SEO position presumably eroding given stale content), **Segment overlap 1** (early-adult prospects researching budgeting / first investing apps might encounter Roadmap before us — but they're not deciding between our RIA and Roadmap, they're deciding between Acorns and M1), **Momentum 1** (clear staleness signals), **Capital backing 1** (solo) → sum **5 → low**.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Coast FIRE Calculator as the single lead magnet.** Specific, named, action-oriented. Compare to generic "Free Financial Planning Guide" lead magnets — Coast FIRE is a specific FI subculture concept that filters for the right prospect.
- **Topic-page architecture for SEO.** "Best Budgeting Apps in 2026 (Free & Paid Mint Alternatives)" + "Top 5 Budget Apps for Couples in 2021 (Better Marriage!)" + "Best Money Market Accounts" + "Best High Yield Savings Accounts" — each post is a long-tail SEO target with year-and-keyword-stuffed titles. Standard playbook in the affiliate-blogger category, executed competently.
- **Personal narrative as differentiation.** Quit job 2018, sold home, RV-traveled 3 years across 38 states, settled in Florida. The narrative threads through the site copy and gives the brand a personal anchor that pure-affiliate sites lack.
- **Honest disclaimer.** "I am not a personal finance professional" is rare in the category — most personal-finance bloggers either claim professional status falsely or hide the disclaimer in a small footer link. Roadmap puts it where someone scanning the page will see it.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **Staleness is the headline.** ©2023 + 2020-2022 most-popular posts + no visible recent posts in the captured sample. SEO position will continue eroding as Google demotes stale-content pages.
- **No paid product** beyond affiliate commissions. No course, no community, no consulting. Revenue is capped at affiliate volume × commission rate × CPC competitiveness.
- **Affiliate-marketing model creates conflict** — the disclaimer addresses this but doesn't resolve it. "Best Money Market Accounts" lists are influenced by which providers pay the highest affiliate commissions, not by which products are actually best.
- **Single-channel revenue dependency.** All eggs in the SEO-and-affiliate basket. If Google penalizes affiliate-heavy sites in a future algorithm update (which they do periodically), Roadmap's revenue model collapses.

## What this means for our entry

Roadmap is **not on our threat radar**. The structural lessons are mostly negative — what *not* to do:

1. **Don't run a stale site.** A 2026 site with "©2023" in the footer and most-popular posts from 2020-2022 is a credibility-erosion machine. Set a quarterly review of all date-stamped surfaces.
2. **Don't depend on affiliate revenue if we're trying to be a fiduciary.** The conflict is structural — even with a clear disclaimer, a "Best [Provider] for [Use Case]" article that ranks the highest-commission product first will erode trust over time.
3. **Don't be afraid to publish honest disclaimers.** "I am not a personal finance professional" is the right thing to say if you're not one. We are an RIA, so our version is different — "We are SEC-registered fiduciaries and these specific facts about us are verifiable: [CRD + ADV link + DRP count]." Direct beats euphemism.

What we might selectively take: the **named lead-magnet pattern** (Coast FIRE Calculator as a specific, action-oriented sign-up incentive) is a useful template even though Coast FIRE itself isn't our target concept.

The narrowest single takeaway: **the catalog now has multiple examples of "personal-finance content site monetized via affiliate or course revenue" (Roadmap, ClearValue Investing, Sven Carlin, Karlton Dennis, Heritage Wealth Planning's content tier).** Worth a `_context/taxonomy.md` note on this as a recognized sub-category. None of them are direct competitors for advisory clients — but together they shape what an early-adult prospect's expectations look like when they first encounter our site.
