# Stash Wealth — Summary

> NYC virtual fee-only RIA founded by **Priya Malani** — former Merrill Lynch HNW advisor, former professional ballerina. Targets six-figure-earning 30-somethings (HENRYs — *High Earners, Not Rich Yet*). **$51.7M AUM, 2 advisors, 3 employees, 0 DRPs** — smallest firm in this batch. Core product: **"The Stash Plan"** — a $5,000 flat-fee, 4-week deep dive into client finances. Brand voice is deliberately edgy: H1 reads *"Get Your Financial Sh*t Together."* Personality-led around Priya.

## The headline

Stash Wealth is the catalog's clearest example of a **single-founder personality-led advisory** for a specific generational niche. The full firm is Priya Malani plus 1 additional advisor and 1 supporting employee per ADV — $51.7M AUM at that headcount means roughly $17M per person, which is small. But the brand reach far exceeds the AUM: Priya is the front person, the founder origin story (parents' financial stress / Merrill Lynch background / pro-ballerina-to-finance arc) is the credibility anchor, and the niche (six-figure 30-somethings) is unusually well-named. Most RIAs that target "young professionals" describe their ICP genericly; Stash names them — *HENRYs* — and owns the term.

The flat-fee product is also unusually transparent: **$5,000 for a 4-week engagement**, no tiers, no AUM percentage, no quote-on-call. That's a real published price for a real defined deliverable. Most fee-only RIAs in our catalog don't publish a number this concrete this prominently.

Threat to USWM is **low** — Stash targets a wealthier, lifestyle-focused 30-something cohort, not USWM's early-career first-investor segment or our 40-50 401(k) holders. But Stash is one of the catalog's strongest examples of **how to brand a generational niche**, which is directly relevant to USWM's two-segment positioning.

## What they sell, to whom

**Single core product**: **The Stash Plan** — $5,000 flat-fee, 4-week deep dive into the client's finances, life, and goals. One-on-one with a dedicated advisor. Combines "cutting-edge financial tools" (the firm's positioning vs. robo-advisors) with "deeply human insights" (vs. pure DIY apps).

**ICP — explicitly named**: *"HENRYs"* — High Earners, Not Rich Yet. Six-figure income, 30-something, "thriving in your career and living life on your terms" but unclear on whether they're doing their finances right. The "Home Of The HENRYs" framing is the central positioning move.

**Positioning copy**:
- "Get Your Financial Sh*t Together" (homepage H1)
- "We specialize in the challenges, and goals of people just like you—we know you're not looking for basic advice."
- "We use the same cutting-edge financial tools as the big firms, but we don't hide behind jargon or robo-advisors."
- Money reframed as "a source of empowerment and joy" rather than stress

## How big a threat

**Low.** **Reach 1** ($51.7M AUM is small, no Knowledge_From_Source captured yet, personality-led so reach scales with Priya's media surface), **Segment overlap 2** (HENRY 30-somethings has some adjacency with USWM's early-career first-investor segment — both are young professionals — but Stash's clients are at a more advanced career stage and higher income), **Momentum 2** (strong brand voice + clear product + recognizable founder), **Capital backing 1** (founder-led, no external) → sum **6 → low**.

## What they're doing well (worth studying)

- **Owning a generational niche by naming it.** "HENRY" is a real term in personal finance. Stash claims it. Most RIAs describe their ICP genericly ("young professionals"); Stash gives them a label that becomes self-identifying.
- **Single product, one price, four weeks.** The Stash Plan is the cleanest service productization in the catalog: $5,000 flat, 4-week timebox, one defined deliverable. Nobody quote-shops you when the price is on the page.
- **Edgy brand voice that fits the audience.** "Get Your Financial Sh*t Together" wouldn't work for a 60-year-old retiree audience. For a 30-something HENRY who's seen the same generic financial-advisor messaging their whole adult life, the swearing reads as authentic.
- **Founder origin story as the trust anchor.** Pro-ballerina-turned-Merrill-Lynch-turned-founder is a memorable arc. The "watching my parents stressed despite Dad's high income" framing connects directly to the HENRY anxiety (high income, low confidence).
- **Reframing money emotionally.** "Money as a source of empowerment and joy" instead of "stress and complexity" — most financial advisors lead with anxiety. Stash leads with permission.

## Where they're vulnerable

- **Single-founder key-person risk.** $51.7M AUM with 2 advisors and 3 employees is fragile. If Priya steps back, the brand collapses. The "leadership team" reference is thin.
- **AUM is small for the brand reach.** Brand visibility exceeds firm scale; the funnel may not yet convert at the rate the brand suggests it should.
- **Single-product strategy has a ceiling.** $5,000 × N clients per year is a hard income cap unless they layer ongoing AUM management — which they may do, but it's not the public-facing pitch.
- **Edgy voice ages.** The "Sh*t Together" hook works in 2026 but may read dated in 3-5 years. Brands that lean on tonal-shock have to refresh more often than brands that lean on substance.
- **No 401(k) optimization angle** — strange omission given the HENRY niche typically has a 401(k) at a tech/finance/professional-services employer.

## What this means for our entry

1. **Name USWM's segments.** "Early adults" and "40-50 mid-career 401(k) holders" are accurate descriptors, but they're not branded. Stash teaches the value of a label that the ICP self-identifies with. For our 40-50 cohort: candidates like *"401(k) Veterans"* / *"Career-Half-Done Investors"* / *"Pre-Retirement Realists"*. For early-adults: *"First-Account Founders"* / *"Roth Builders"*. Worth A/B testing — not just describing the segment.
2. **Publish a flat-fee product alongside AUM.** Stash's $5,000 4-week deep dive is a teachable structure. USWM's equivalent could be a **"Foundation Plan"** at a published flat fee for the early-adult segment — low enough to be a no-brainer entry, structured enough that the deliverable is clear. Captures prospects too small for AUM-based engagement.
3. **Lean into Kean's and Beata's origin stories.** Kean's wirehouse arc (Merrill / JPMorgan / Citi / UBS / AIG → independent) is the same shape as Priya's (Merrill → independent). It's a credibility-by-credentialed-departure story: *"I worked at the big firms. I left to do it right."* USWM should tell this more explicitly on the About page.
4. **Don't copy the edgy voice.** USWM's brand voice is *confident, not boastful · honest broker · editorial, not corporate · quiet authority* (per `_context/brand_voice.md`). The Stash framing would clash. Stash is a useful tonal *contrast* for USWM — they're proof you can win a 30-something audience without quiet authority, but USWM doesn't compete on that axis.

The narrowest single takeaway: **name the segments USWM serves, and publish a flat-fee entry product.** Both moves are visible in Stash's strongest positioning work.
